## 📝 Personal Notes

### Strengths
- Exceptionally concise — can be read in a single sitting
- Fills the practical gap left by Ahrens' abstract approach
- Shows actual note examples (rare in this genre)
- Tool-agnostic philosophy helps future-proof the advice
- The keyword strategy ("writer vs. archivist") is genuinely insightful
- Matching rituals to energy levels is a unique and practical contribution

### Weaknesses / Criticisms
- Very short — more of an extended essay than a book
- Light on research/evidence; experience-based only
- Some reviewers felt it was too basic for intermediate/advanced users
- Doesn't deeply explore Luhmann's original method
- The "examples" promised in the title are limited
- Feels like a long blog post expanded into a book

### Key Quotes
- "The Zettelkasten method is a way of organizing paper in a non-hierarchical way. The purpose of a Zettelkasten is not to find an individual note, but rather to explore the connections amongst notes."
- "Choose keywords that are specific to an idea, and unique from generic ideas. Think like a writer, not an archivist."
- "The act of re-writing fosters deeper understanding and clarity, encouraging a more profound engagement with material."

### Position in the Note-Taking Canon
- **Easiest entry point** among Zettelkasten books
- Less theoretical than Ahrens, less system-heavy than Forte
- Best used as a **companion** to *How to Take Smart Notes* (Ahrens for theory, Kadavy for practice)

### See Also
- **Author**: David Kadavy — also wrote *Mind Management, Not Time Management*, *Design for Hackers*
- **Complement**: *How to Take Smart Notes* by Sönke Ahrens
- **Complement**: *A System for Writing* by Bob Doto
- **Alternative**: *Building a Second Brain* by Tiago Forte (more comprehensive digital system)
- **Tools**: Obsidian, Roam Research, Logseq, plain text editors
