## 📝 Personal Notes

### Strengths
- First comprehensive English treatment of Luhmann's Zettelkasten
- Strongly grounded in cognitive science (elaboration, spacing, retrieval practice)
- The four principles and six steps provide a clear conceptual framework
- Reframes note-taking from a passive storage activity to an active thinking process

### Weaknesses / Criticisms
- **Abstract**: Light on specific implementation details for digital tools
- **Repetitive**: Some readers find the core ideas restated too often
- **Niche Focus**: Written primarily for academics and nonfiction writers
- **No App Guidance**: Doesn't address Obsidian, Roam, Notion, etc.
- **Overpromises**: "Writing will practically write itself" can feel unrealistic in practice

### Key Quotes
- "Writing is not the outcome of thinking; it is the medium in which thinking takes place."
- "The slip-box is not a collection of notes. Working with it is less about retrieving specific notes and more about being pointed to relevant facts and generating insight by letting ideas mingle."
- "A good structure enables you to move from one task to another seamlessly."
- "If you can't write a concise summary of an idea in your own words, you don't understand it."

### See Also
- **Book**: *Digital Zettelkasten* by David Kadavy — practical implementation
- **Book**: *A System for Writing* by Bob Doto — Zettelkasten for writing output
- **Book**: *Building a Second Brain* by Tiago Forte — modern digital adaptation
- **Person**: Niklas Luhmann — originator of the method
- **Tool**: Obsidian, Roam Research, Logseq — popular digital Zettelkasten tools
