## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

The single best predictor of academic success is not intelligence but the use of effective study strategies — and note-taking is the most important of these. Effective note-taking is not about recording information but about *selecting* what matters and *connecting* it to existing knowledge. By understanding how working memory, cognitive load, and elaboration work, you can choose the right strategy for each learning situation: highlighting for structure identification, summaries for compression, concept maps for connection, and elaborated notes for deep understanding.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. The Two Core Processes of Note-Taking
| Process | What It Is | Strategies |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| **Selection** | Identifying what's important | Highlighting, headings, summaries |
| **Connection** | Relating new info to existing knowledge | Elaboration, concept maps |

### 2. Selection Strategies
| Strategy | Best For | Limitation |
|----------|----------|------------|
| Highlighting | Flagging key passages | Passive; doesn't guarantee understanding |
| Headings | Exposing text structure | Only works with well-structured texts |
| Text Summaries | Compressing main ideas | Requires selection skill |
| Graphic Summaries | Visual relationships | Time-intensive to create |

### 3. Connection Strategies
| Strategy | Mechanism | Effectiveness |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Elaboration | Linking new to known | High — deep processing |
| Concept Maps | Explicit relationship mapping | High — visual + structural |
| Questions | Self-testing and retrieval | High — active recall |

### 4. Working Memory Model (Foundation)
- Working memory is limited (7±2 items)
- Cognitive load (intrinsic + extrinsic) determines strategy effectiveness
- Individual WMC differences affect which strategy works best

### 5. The Contiguity Effect
Learning is better when related information is presented close together (spatially or temporally). This has direct implications for note formatting.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

1. **Working Memory** — The limited-capacity system that holds and manipulates information during learning.
2. **Cognitive Load** — The demand placed on working memory by learning material; intrinsic (difficulty) + extrinsic (presentation).
3. **Elaboration** — Connecting new information to existing knowledge; the most effective learning strategy.
4. **Selection** — The process of identifying important information; the first step in effective note-taking.
5. **Contiguity Effect** — Presenting related information close together improves learning.
6. **Concept Map** — A visual diagram showing relationships between concepts (propositions).
7. **Mind Map** — A radial, association-based diagram; differs from concept maps in structure and purpose.
8. **Signal vs. Noise** — The distinction between important and unimportant information; selection skill.
9. **Advance Organizer** — Pre-reading material that primes the reader for content structure.
10. **Verbatim Effect** — The finding that verbatim note-taking produces poorer learning than paraphrased notes.
11. **Multimedia Learning** — Learning from words and pictures; principles for effective combination.
12. **Individual Differences** — Working memory capacity and learning style variations that affect strategy choice.
