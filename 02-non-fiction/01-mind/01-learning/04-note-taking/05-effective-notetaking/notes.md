## 📝 Personal Notes

### Strengths
- **Most research-grounded** note-taking book available
- Fully referenced with cognitive psychology studies
- Clear distinction between selection and connection — a powerful framework
- Workbook format with exercises forces active engagement
- Covers the science of working memory and cognitive load thoroughly
- Includes multimedia learning principles (3rd ed)
- The strategy selection matrix (Ch 13) alone is worth the price

### Weaknesses / Criticisms
- **Dry, textbook style** — not an easy read; feels like homework
- **No digital tool coverage** — methods are tool-agnostic but feel dated
- **Mnemonics excluded** (pushed to another book in the series)
- **Skimming not covered** — readers wanted more on this
- **Student-focused** — less useful for knowledge workers outside academia
- **Exercises interrupt flow** — works better as a reference than a cover-to-cover read
- **Goodreads presence very small** — hard to gauge public reception

### Key Quotes
- "You can predict how well a student will do simply on the basis of their use of effective study strategies."
- "The first and most crucial part of effective note-taking is about selecting what information is important."
- "Verbatim notes are of minimal value, unless you are simply using them as a stepping stone."
- "Effective strategies are often those you are initially *not* particularly comfortable with."

### Reviews Summary
- *Zettelkasten.de*: "The book is not about good atomic notes. It is about the effects of various ways of note-taking."
- *Goodreads reviewer*: "The last chapter itself, with a summary of the book, is worth every penny."
- *Paul (customer testimonial)*: "Note-taking provides the framework for learning."

### Position in the Note-Taking Canon
- **Most rigorous** — strongest research foundation
- **Least popular** — smallest readership among the six books in this category
- **Best complement** to the Zettelkasten books (Ahrens, Kadavy, Doto)
- **Essential for students**; supplementary for knowledge workers
