# Remember It! — Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

**Reading Guidance:** Read this book if you frequently forget names, passwords, grocery lists, or where you left your keys, and want a fun, illustrated, no-nonsense guide to fixing it. Skip this if you're looking for academic theory on memory neuroscience (read Higbee's *Your Memory* instead) or a narrative account of memory championships (read *Moonwalking with Einstein*).

---

## Front Matter

### Foreword by Dr. Sanjay Gupta
- **Core Question:** Why should you trust a memory champion?
- **What it Covers:** Gupta explains how he met Dellis and was amazed by his practical approach. He vouches for the techniques as a neurosurgeon, noting they align with how the brain naturally works.
- **Key Concepts:** Neuroplasticity, practical application
- **Takeaway:** These techniques are medically sound, not parlor tricks.

### My Story and Why You Should Listen to Me (Chapter 1)
- **Core Question:** How did an ordinary person become a USA Memory Champion?
- **What it Covers:** Dellis's personal origin story—his grandmother's Alzheimer's diagnosis, his first exposure to memory techniques, his journey to the USA Memory Championship, and his realization that existing books were too dry or competition-focused.
- **Key Concepts:** Motivation, Alzheimer's, neuroplasticity, "memory is trainable"
- **Examples:** His grandmother forgetting him; learning a restaurant menu as a waiter; 16th place in his first competition.
- **Takeaway:** Memory improvement is not about innate talent—it's about technique and practice.
- **Thesis Connection:** Establishes credibility and the book's mission: practical, everyday memory tools.

## Main Content

### Chapter 2: Basic Things You Must Remember Before You Start
- **Core Question:** What are the fundamental principles that all memory techniques are built on?
- **What it Covers:** The SEE-LINK-GO framework. Visualization (creating vivid images), Association (linking new to known), and Location (placing images along a journey). Introduces the Journey Method (Memory Palace), Linking Method, Number-Rhyme Peg System, and the Alphabet Peg System.
- **Key Concepts:** SEE-LINK-GO, Visualization, Association, Location/Journey Method, Peg Systems, Chunking, Attention
- **Examples:** Memorizing a grocery list by walking through your house; using 1=bun, 2=shoe for numbered lists.
- **Takeaway:** Master these 3 principles and you can memorize almost anything.
- **Thesis Connection:** This is the engine room of the entire book. All later chapters are applications of these fundamentals.

### Chapter 3: Brain Farts, Name-Amnesia, and the Everyday Things That Drive Our Memories Mad
- **Core Question:** How do I stop forgetting names, where I put things, and everyday tasks?
- **What it Covers:** The FACE method for names (Focus, Ask, Connect, Endear). Techniques for remembering where you parked your car, where you left your keys, directions, and to-do lists. The concept of "encoding specificity"—making a conscious mental note when placing an item.
- **Key Concepts:** FACE Method, Encoding Specificity, Attention, Association for names
- **Examples:** Meeting someone named "Alice" and visualizing Alice in Wonderland; consciously noting "I am placing my keys on the red table."
- **Takeaway:** Most everyday forgetting is due to lack of attention, not lack of memory capacity.
- **Thesis Connection:** Applies SEE-LINK-GO to the most common real-world memory failures.

### Chapter 4: For All the List-Based Things That Your Mind Wishes It Could Remember Forward and Backward
- **Core Question:** How do I memorize ordered lists of any length?
- **What it Covers:** Deep dive into the Journey Method for long lists. The Linking Method for shorter sequences. The Peg Systems (Number-Rhyme and Alphabet) for numbered lists. Grocery lists, to-do lists, US presidents, speeches, and presentations.
- **Key Concepts:** Journey Method (advanced), Linking Method, Peg Systems (applied), creating effective journeys
- **Examples:** Memorizing all 45 US presidents in order using journeys; memorizing a 20-item grocery list.
- **Takeaway:** Lists are easy once you have a reliable spatial framework (journey) or peg system.
- **Thesis Connection:** Expands Pillar 2 (Journey Method) with detailed application guidance.

### Chapter 5: Making Sense of the Gobbledygook Number Things and How to Make Them Stick
- **Core Question:** How do I remember numbers (phone numbers, PINs, passwords, dates)?
- **What it Covers:** Three systems for number memorization—Number-Shape System (easy), Major System (medium), PAO System (advanced). Practical application to phone numbers, credit cards, passwords, historical dates, and important numbers.
- **Key Concepts:** Number-Shape System, Major System (Phonetic), PAO System, number encoding, creating a PAO list
- **Examples:** Converting 305-399-3026 into images; creating a PAO for 00-99; memorizing a 20-digit password.
- **Takeaway:** Numbers are abstract and hard to remember—convert them to pictures and they become easy.
- **Thesis Connection:** Applies the SEE part of SEE-LINK-GO to the most abstract type of information.

### Chapter 6: Tips for Memorizing Some of the Other Things Your Bumfuzzled Brain Has to Deal With
- **Core Question:** How do I memorize vocabulary, foreign languages, facts, and other non-list/non-number information?
- **What it Covers:** Foreign language vocabulary using the Keyword Method. Memorizing definitions, facts, and trivia. Remembering what you read. Learning names of plants, animals, and scientific terms.
- **Key Concepts:** Keyword Method, Substitute Word Method, Fact memorization, Active reading
- **Examples:** Learning Spanish "gato" (cat) by visualizing a gate with a cat on it; memorizing the 10 longest rivers.
- **Takeaway:** Any piece of information can be "translated" into a memorable image.
- **Thesis Connection:** Demonstrates the universality of SEE-LINK-GO for all information types.

### Chapter 7: The Last Few Things to Make Your Memory an Absolute Memorizing Monster
- **Core Question:** How do I maintain and improve my memory long-term?
- **What it Covers:** Spaced repetition for permanent retention. Brain health: sleep, nutrition, exercise. Mindfulness and concentration training. Curiosity and lifelong learning. Review schedules. Encouraging a "memory lifestyle."
- **Key Concepts:** Spaced Repetition, Review schedules, Brain health, Mindfulness, Neuroplasticity
- **Examples:** Review schedule: 1 hour → 1 day → 1 week → 1 month → 1 year.
- **Takeaway:** Techniques alone aren't enough—you need a brain-healthy lifestyle and consistent practice.
- **Thesis Connection:** Closes the loop: skills + lifestyle = lasting memory improvement.
