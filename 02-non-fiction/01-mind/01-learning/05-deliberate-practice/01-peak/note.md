## 📝 Personal Notes

### Context & Significance
- Written partly as a corrective to Malcolm Gladwell's 10,000-hour rule (from *Outliers*, 2008), which misrepresented Ericsson's original 1993 study on violinists
- Ericsson published an excerpt in Salon titled "Malcolm Gladwell got us wrong" to clarify the oversimplification
- Represents the synthesis of three decades of peer-reviewed research on expert performance

### Core Distinction: Types of Practice
1. **Naive Practice** — Mindless repetition, minimal improvement
2. **Purposeful Practice** — Focused, goal-oriented, feedback-driven, comfort-zone stretching
3. **Deliberate Practice** — The gold standard: teacher-designed, field-anchored, representation-building

### Key Research Studies Referenced
- Sakakibara (2014) — Perfect pitch training in 24 children
- Berlin violinists study (Ericsson et al., 1993) — 10,000-hour differential
- London taxi driver hippocampal growth (Maguire et al., 2000)
- Steve Faloon digit-span memory training

### Strengths
- Authoritative — written by the preeminent researcher in the field
- Well-structured — builds concepts incrementally
- Rich examples span sports, music, chess, medicine

### Weaknesses / Criticisms
- Deliberate practice is hard to apply in fields without established teachers/coaches
- Underemphasizes role of motivation and passion (addressed only briefly)
- Some critics argue it overstates the case against innate ability in domains like height in basketball or IQ in certain STEM fields
- Less actionable for knowledge workers compared to athletes/musicians

### Cross-References
- See **The Talent Code** (Coyle) for the myelin-based neurological model of deep practice
- See **Talent Is Overrated** (Colvin) for a business-focused application of Ericsson's ideas
- See **Grit** (Duckworth) for the motivational/perseverance component less explored in Peak
