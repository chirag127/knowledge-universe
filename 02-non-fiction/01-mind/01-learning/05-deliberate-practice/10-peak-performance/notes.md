## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways

- The universal formula is Stress + Rest = Growth — it applies to body, mind, and creativity
- The best performers do not push at 100% all the time; they cycle intensity (periodization)
- Rest is not passive — it is an active performance-enhancing strategy
- A pre-performance routine (priming) is essential for delivering peak performance on demand
- Constraints and minimalism focus energy on what actually matters
- Purpose beyond self is the most sustainable source of motivation

### How This Connects to Deliberate Practice

Peak Performance provides the missing framework for Anders Ericsson's deliberate practice model. Ericsson focuses almost exclusively on the "stress" side (effortful practice targeting weaknesses), but Stulberg and Magness emphasize that deliberate practice is only sustainable if paired with deliberate rest. The book also adds the psychological dimensions (priming, purpose) that make prolonged deliberate practice possible without burnout.

### Criticism & Limitations

- The concepts are not entirely original (synthesized from existing research)
- Some readers may find it too focused on athletic examples
- The purpose section (Chapters 8-9) is thinner than the growth equation section
- Limited discussion of organizational/systemic factors that affect performance
- The "Performance Practices" boxes are helpful but sometimes interrupt the narrative flow

### Best For

- Athletes and coaches looking for evidence-based training approaches
- Knowledge workers seeking sustainable productivity
- Creatives who struggle with burnout
- Anyone practicing deliberate practice who needs a recovery framework
- Leaders building high-performance organizational cultures
