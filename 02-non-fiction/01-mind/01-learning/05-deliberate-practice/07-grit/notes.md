## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways

- Effort counts twice — it builds skill AND converts skill into achievement
- The four psychological assets of grit are: Interest, Practice, Purpose, Hope
- Grit can be cultivated both internally (through the four assets) and externally (through parenting, activities, and culture)
- The "Hard Thing Rule" is a practical tool: everyone must do one hard thing daily, you can quit only at natural break points, and you choose your own hard thing
- Culture is the most powerful external force for building grit

### How This Connects to Deliberate Practice

Duckworth draws heavily on Anders Ericsson's deliberate practice research. The "Practice" chapter (Chapter 7) is essentially an accessible introduction to deliberate practice principles: specific goals, full concentration, immediate feedback, and operating at the edge of one's ability. Grit provides the motivational engine that sustains deliberate practice over years.

### Criticism & Limitations

- Some researchers argue grit is just the "Conscientiousness" facet of the Big Five personality traits, not a distinct construct
- The emphasis on perseverance could be harmful if it encourages people to persist in dead-end situations
- Range (David Epstein) directly challenges grit, arguing that quitting (sampling broadly) is often more adaptive
- Duckworth's data relies heavily on self-report (Grit Scale), which may not correlate perfectly with actual behavior
- Most evidence is correlational, not causal
