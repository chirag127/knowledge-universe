# Why Don't Students Like School?: A Cognitive Scientist Answers Questions About How the Mind Works and How It Relates to the Classroom

## Metadata
* **Authors**: Daniel T. Willingham
* **Published**: 2009 (2nd Edition published in 2020)
* **Edition**: 2nd (Jossey-Bass)
* **Genre**: Learning Science / Educational Psychology / Cognitive Science
* **Description**: A translation of cognitive science research into practical classroom principles. The book addresses why the human brain avoids thinking, the necessity of factual knowledge for critical thought, the memory benefits of narrative structures, the limits of abstraction, and why popular concepts like visual/auditory learning styles are scientific myths.
* **Companion Books**: *Make It Stick* (Brown et al.), *How We Learn* (Carey), *Powerful Teaching* (Agarwal & Bain)

## Should You Read the Full Book?

**Read the full book if** you are a teacher, homeschooler, or educational designer looking for a comprehensive guide to structure your teaching plans. Willingham writes with extreme clarity, organizing each chapter around a common teacher query (e.g., "Is Drilling Worth It?") and concluding with a dedicated list of direct implications for classroom design. The book contains useful visual models of human memory, classroom checklists, and highly relatable analogies (such as comparing learning styles to matching styles of coats) that help teachers bridge cognitive theory and real-world instruction.

**Use the summaries instead if** you are a self-directed learner or professional looking for core cognitive principles to optimize your own study habits. While the book's cognitive science is universal, its framing and suggestions are heavily tailored to K-12 teachers managing classrooms of 30 students. If you do not need tips on classroom management, lesson planning, grading systems, or how to design technology policies for schools, these summaries will isolate the core cognitive models (like the Working Memory bottleneck, the role of background knowledge, and the power of stories) for your personal study without the school-centric instructions.

> **Verdict**: *Read the full book if you are a K-12 teacher, tutor, or parent looking for a structured, classroom-friendly application of cognitive psychology. Use the summaries if you want a direct, personal blueprint of the learning science without the school-system framing.*
