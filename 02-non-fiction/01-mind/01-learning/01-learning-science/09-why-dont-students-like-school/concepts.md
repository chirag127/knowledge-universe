# Key Concepts Glossary: Why Don't Students Like School?

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

---

## 🧠 Brain Biology & Cognitive Load

### Cognitive Avoidance
- **Definition**: The brain's natural evolutionary tendency to avoid slow, effortful logical reasoning (thinking) in favor of fast, low-energy processes like memory recall and sensory automation.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 1, where the author asserts that the brain is not designed for thinking, but rather designed to avoid it.
- **Real-World Application**: Avoid cognitive fatigue when learning a complex system by breaking study sessions into short, high-focus intervals rather than long marathons.

### Joy of Resolution
- **Definition**: The pleasurable emotional and neurological response (dopamine release) triggered when a person successfully solves a cognitive challenge of moderate difficulty.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 1 as the primary source of natural curiosity and student engagement in schools.
- **Real-World Application**: Keep yourself motivated when learning to code by solving small, bite-sized algorithm exercises that offer quick, satisfying feedback.

### Curiosity Boundary
- **Definition**: The sweet spot of task difficulty where a problem is challenging enough to prevent boredom, but solvable enough to prevent immediate frustration.
- **In the Book**: Described in Chapter 1 as the zone of difficulty where students enjoy thinking.
- **Real-World Application**: If you find yourself giving up on a textbook, lower the difficulty by reading introductory summaries or watching an overview video first.

### Working Memory Bottleneck
- **Definition**: The hard biological limitation on the amount of information the conscious mind can hold and manipulate simultaneously (typically estimated at $7 \pm 2$ items or fewer).
- **In the Book**: Discussed across chapters as the primary constraint on human learning, reasoning, and comprehension.
- **Real-World Application**: Use scratchpads, outline lists, or digital whiteboards to store intermediate steps of a problem, protecting your working memory from overload.

## 📚 Memory & Knowledge Representation

### Background Knowledge Compound Effect
- **Definition**: The cognitive reality that the more factual knowledge stored in your long-term memory, the easier it is to encode, comprehend, and recall new, related information.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 2 to show why factual memorization is a prerequisite for critical thinking and reading comprehension.
- **Real-World Application**: Read introductory articles on a subject before attending a advanced lecture; the background facts will act as "hooks" to capture the lecture details.

### Chunking
- **Definition**: The cognitive process of grouping separate, individual pieces of data into a single, cohesive unit of meaning, reducing working memory load.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 2 as a primary benefit of background knowledge, allowing experts to hold complex architectures in mind.
- **Real-World Application**: A senior programmer reads 5 lines of code and chunks it as a "database connection query," while a novice struggles to read each variable name.

### Memory as the Residue of Thought
- **Definition**: The cognitive law stating that you remember what you actively pay attention to and contemplate during an event, not what you merely see or hear.
- **In the Book**: Featured in Chapter 3 to explain why flashy but conceptually shallow class activities fail to produce long-term retention.
- **Real-World Application**: When studying a diagram, do not just stare at it; ask yourself "why is this component connected here?" to force semantic processing.

### The Four Cs of Storytelling
- **Definition**: A cognitive framework organizing information around Character, Conflict, Complications, and Causality, making it highly memorable for the human brain.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 3 as the structure that makes television or narratives highly memorable compared to abstract lectures.
- **Real-World Application**: Explain a dry technical system by framing the data packets as "characters" trying to navigate a "conflict" (network congestion) to reach a goal.

## 🧱 Abstraction & Automation

### Shallow Structure
- **Definition**: The literal, superficial features and concrete story elements of a problem or scenario.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 4 as the only features that novices perceive when looking at a new problem.
- **Real-World Application**: A novice sees a coding exercise about "calculating grocery totals" and thinks it is about shopping, failing to see the underlying loop concept.

### Deep Structure
- **Definition**: The underlying conceptual principle, formula, or relationship that governs a problem, independent of its superficial details.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 4, showing that experts focus on deep structures automatically, allowing for flexible knowledge transfer.
- **Real-World Application**: Recognize that a physics problem about a falling weight and a chemistry problem about gas expansion share the same deep thermodynamic laws.

### Cognitive Scaffolding
- **Definition**: A teaching method that uses familiar, concrete analogies and step-by-step examples as temporary supports to guide a learner toward understanding abstract structures.
- **In the Book**: Recommended in Chapter 4 as the primary way to teach abstract concepts to K-12 students.
- **Real-World Application**: Learn the concept of web routing by comparing it to physical mail delivery and post offices before diving into technical port rules.

### Automation (Cognitive)
- **Definition**: The cognitive transition of a skill or subskill from conscious, effortful working memory control to effortless, automatic retrieval from long-term memory.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 5 as the primary benefit of practice (drilling), freeing up working memory for creative problem-solving.
- **Real-World Application**: Practice typing or keyboard shortcuts until they are automatic, allowing you to focus 100% of your cognitive capacity on the text content.

### Expert Schemas
- **Definition**: Dense, highly integrated mental networks of principles, patterns, and relationships in long-term memory acquired over a decade of practice.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 6 to explain why novices cannot think like professional scientists or historians without years of foundation building.
- **Real-World Application**: Respect that a senior architect's intuition is built on a massive database of past failures and structural patterns, which cannot be copied overnight.

## ⚡ Modality, Intelligence, & Practice

### Learning Styles Myth
- **Definition**: The disconfirmed educational theory that matching instruction to a student's dominant sensory modality (Visual, Auditory, Kinesthetic) improves learning.
- **In the Book**: Addressed in Chapter 7 as a pervasive myth that lacks empirical evidence and wastes teacher resources.
- **Real-World Application**: Stop searching for "auditory" coding tutorials if you are struggling; instead, find tutorials that match the conceptual meaning of the code.

### Content-Modality Alignment
- **Definition**: The practice of aligning the sensory format of instruction (visual, auditory, physical) with the inherent meaning of the subject matter, rather than student preferences.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 7 as the scientific alternative to learning styles; all students learn geography best via maps (visual) and pronunciation via audio (auditory).
- **Real-World Application**: When studying a database structure, use a visual schema map because the relationships are inherently spatial, benefiting all learners.

### Malleable Intelligence
- **Definition**: The cognitive reality that intelligence (IQ) is not a fixed genetic trait, but can be developed through environment, education, and effort.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 8, citing the Flynn Effect and the neurological impact of environmental enrichment.
- **Real-World Application**: Reframe academic struggles by telling yourself "I haven't mastered this concept *yet*," rather than assuming you lack the intelligence to learn it.

### Process Praise
- **Definition**: Praising a student's strategies, effort, and focus rather than their natural talent or final test scores to foster a growth mindset.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 8 as a critical technique to build resilience in slow learners and protect against the fragility of fixed mindsets.
- **Real-World Application**: When evaluating your own progress, praise yourself for sticking to your study schedule rather than focusing on getting a perfect score.

### Technological Distraction
- **Definition**: The state where flashy visual, interactive, or game-like elements in educational software overload working memory with non-essential cognitive load.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 9 to show how classroom technology can inadvertently direct attention away from the core concept.
- **Real-World Application**: Choose clean, text-based study apps over flashy, gamified platforms that distract you with popups, animations, and point systems.

### Pedagogical Deliberate Practice
- **Definition**: Goal-directed, feedback-intensive practice designed to systematically improve specific teaching methods and classroom strategies.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 10 as the primary way teachers can manage their own professional growth and avoid stagnation.
- **Real-World Application**: Audit your presentation skills by recording yourself presenting, reviewing the footage to count filler words, and drilling transitions.
