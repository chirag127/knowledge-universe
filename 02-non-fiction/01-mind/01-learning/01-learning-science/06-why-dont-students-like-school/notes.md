## 📝 Personal Notes

### Introduction

The book bridges cognitive psychology research and classroom practice. Willingham selected each principle based on four criteria: (1) it is true in all contexts, (2) backed by extensive data, (3) has a significant impact on learning, and (4) suggests novel classroom applications teachers wouldn't otherwise know.

### Chapter 1: Why Don't Students Like School?

The mind is designed to avoid thinking. People enjoy *successful* thinking at the right difficulty level. Curiosity is fragile—too hard or too easy kills it. The teacher's job: frame content as solvable problems that give the dopamine hit of successful cognition.

### Chapter 2: Facts First, Then Skills

The single most impactful idea in the book for curriculum design. Background knowledge is not the "lower" part of Bloom's taxonomy to be rushed through—it is the *foundation* that makes the "higher" levels possible. The Matthew Effect means early gaps compound. Teach deep, connected content.

### Chapter 3: Memory Is the Residue of Thought

The single most useful instructional design principle. Every lesson plan should be screened through: "What will students be thinking about during this activity?" If the answer isn't the target content, redesign. This explains why well-intentioned "engaging" activities often fail.

### Chapter 4: Abstract Ideas

You cannot teach abstraction directly. You build it by giving students multiple concrete examples that share deep structure. Understanding is analogical—new knowledge maps onto old. Patience: deep knowledge takes time; don't pretend shallow knowledge is sufficient.

### Chapter 5: Drilling

Reframes practice from drudgery to liberation. Automaticity is what makes higher-order thinking possible. The key insight: you can't think creatively about something if the basics are consuming all your working memory. Want creativity? Automatize the fundamentals first.

### Chapter 6: Experts vs. Novices

Explains why "think like a scientist" curricula often fail. Experts don't just know more—they *perceive* differently. Asking novices to emulate expert reasoning without the underlying knowledge base is futile. Teach understanding; expertise follows.

### Chapter 7: Learning Styles

The clearest debunking of learning styles in print. The evidence is overwhelming: modality matching does not improve outcomes. This is not a "both sides" issue. The implication: stop labeling students, start analyzing what the content itself demands visually, aurally, or kinesthetically.

### Chapter 8: Slow Learners and Intelligence

The Flynn Effect proves IQ is not fixed. Praise effort, not ability. Normalize failure. Teach study skills—don't assume students know how to learn. Honest acknowledgment: catching up requires massive effort, but it is possible.

### Chapter 9: Technology (2nd Edition Only)

The most practical framework for evaluating edtech: ask the same cognitive question. Does it change what students think about? Does it reduce extraneous load? Does it enable forms of practice or feedback otherwise impossible? The "digital native" is a myth.

### Chapter 10: What About My Mind?

Teachers must apply the same principles to themselves. Deliberate practice > experience alone. Seek feedback. Observe others. Keep a diary. Teaching is a cognitive skill—improve it the same way students improve theirs.

### Conclusion

"Education makes better minds, and knowledge of the mind can make better education." The principles are summarized in a master table. The book ends by reminding teachers that cognitive science is descriptive, not prescriptive—it illuminates constraints and possibilities, but teaching remains a craft.

---

**Key Takeaway:** The book's power lies in its unification of scattered cognitive science findings into a coherent, actionable framework. The principle "memory is the residue of thought" alone is worth the price—it fundamentally reframes lesson planning from presentation to cognitive engagement.
