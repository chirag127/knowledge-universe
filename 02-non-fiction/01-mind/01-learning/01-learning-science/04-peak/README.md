# Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise

## Metadata
* **Authors**: K. Anders Ericsson, Robert Pool
* **Published**: 2016
* **Edition**: 1st (Eamon Dolan / Houghton Mifflin Harcourt)
* **Genre**: Learning Science / Performance Psychology / Expertise Research
* **Description**: The definitive scientific work on expertise, written by the world's foremost researcher on expert performance. Ericsson spent 30+ years studying elite performers across chess, music, medicine, athletics, and memory — and this book presents his unified theory: expert performance is not the product of innate talent, but of a specific form of structured, feedback-driven practice called **deliberate practice**.
* **Companion Books**: *A Mind for Numbers* (Oakley), *Outliers* (Gladwell — popular misrepresentation of Ericsson's research), *Grit* (Duckworth), *Talent Is Overrated* (Colvin)

## Should You Read the Full Book?

**Read the full book if** you are seriously committed to developing expertise in a specific domain — music, athletics, programming, medicine, chess, or any performance field. *Peak* is the source text: Ericsson is the researcher whose 30 years of empirical studies are cited by every other book on expertise. The Berlin violinist study, the Steve Faloon memory experiments, and the London taxi driver research are explained in their full scientific context here, with important nuance that popularizations like *Outliers* stripped out. If you want to understand deliberate practice well enough to design your own training program, read the full book.

**Use the summaries instead if** you have already read Gladwell's *Outliers* and are looking to correct or deepen your understanding of the 10,000-hour concept, or if your interest is in the high-level frameworks (naive vs. purposeful vs. deliberate practice, mental representations, the 4-stage developmental trajectory) rather than the research methodology. The book is occasionally repetitive and its treatment of "soft" professional domains is less developed than its treatment of structured performance fields. The summaries here capture the complete conceptual framework.

> **Verdict**: *Designing a serious training program or working in a performance domain (music, sports, surgery) → read the full book. Wanting the conceptual framework to improve general learning → the summaries here are sufficient.*
