# Key Concepts Glossary: Novice to Expert

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

---

## 💡 Core Philosophy & Competence

### Accelerated Learning
- **Definition**: A strategic approach to skill acquisition that focuses on maximizing learning speed and cognitive efficiency by targeting core principles.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 1 as the first core concept, serving as the foundation of the S.J. Scott learning method.
- **Real-World Application**: Learn a new software tool by studying only the basic commands that allow you to produce your first output within an hour.

### 80/20 Rule (Pareto Principle)
- **Definition**: The principle stating that roughly 80% of the results in any domain come from 20% of the effort, inputs, or subskills.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 1 to help learners identify the high-yield microcomponents of a skill to master first.
- **Real-World Application**: When learning web development, focus on HTML/CSS layouts and basic Javascript (the 20%) to build 80% of standard landing pages.

### Just-in-Time (JIT) Learning
- **Definition**: An information management strategy where a learner acquires only the specific knowledge required to complete a current, immediate task.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 2 as the second core concept, contrasted with "Just-in-Case" study.
- **Real-World Application**: Instead of reading an entire SQL manual, search only for the "JOIN" query syntax you need to link two database tables today.

### Just-in-Case Learning
- **Definition**: The highly inefficient habit of stockpiling information, books, courses, and notes that you *might* need in the future, leading to information overload.
- **In the Book**: Contrasted with JIT learning in Chapter 2 as a primary source of student procrastination.
- **Real-World Application**: Stop buying online courses on data structures that you have no immediate plans or projects to use.

### Information Paralysis
- **Definition**: The state of mental indecision and inaction caused by being overwhelmed by too many resource options, courses, or guides.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 2 and 6 as a common pitfall that prevents beginners from starting a skill.
- **Real-World Application**: Avoid scrolling endlessly through tutorial lists; select one course, hide all other tabs, and start studying.

## 📈 The Stages of Competency

### Unconscious Incompetence
- **Definition**: The initial stage of learning where an individual does not understand or know how to do something, and is unaware of their own ignorance.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 3 as the first level of the four stages of competence.
- **Real-World Application**: A beginner writer who thinks writing is easy because they have never attempted to construct a long, structured essay.

### Conscious Incompetence
- **Definition**: The second stage of learning where an individual recognizes their knowledge gaps and understands what skills they need to develop.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 3 as the stage where learners experience the most frustration and are prone to quitting.
- **Real-World Application**: A beginner programmer who realizes they do not know how to write an API and feels overwhelmed by the complexity of server requests.

### Conscious Competence
- **Definition**: The third stage of learning where an individual knows how to execute a skill, but execution requires high conscious effort, focus, and working memory space.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 3 as the stage of effortful practice.
- **Real-World Application**: A student driver who can park a car perfectly but must consciously think about mirrors, speed, and wheel angle.

### Unconscious Competence
- **Definition**: The final stage of mastery where a skill has been practiced so thoroughly that it becomes an automatic habit, executed by the subconscious with minimal effort.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 3 as the ultimate goal of skill acquisition.
- **Real-World Application**: An experienced touch-typist who writes sentences at 80 words per minute without looking at or consciously thinking about the keys.

## 🎯 Skill Selection & Sourcing

### Learning Style Preference
- **Definition**: An individual's subjective favorite modality (visual, auditory, social) for engaging with study materials, useful for maintaining motivation.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 4 as Step 1, helping to reduce study friction.
- **Real-World Application**: If you find reading textbooks dry, select interactive coding challenges or video guides to keep yourself engaged.

### Singular Focus Commitment
- **Definition**: The discipline of dedicating 100% of your scheduled study blocks to a single target skill for a set period, tabling all other learning projects.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 5 as Step 2 to prevent the fragmentation of learning capacity.
- **Real-World Application**: Commit to learning React for the next 3 months; do not start studying Spanish or guitar until the time block is over.

### Multi-learning Fragility
- **Definition**: The failure to acquire any skill due to trying to learn multiple, unrelated complex subjects simultaneously, which spreads attention too thin.
- **In the Book**: Contrasted with singular focus in Chapter 5.
- **Real-World Application**: Stop attempting to learn three programming languages at the same time; focus on mastering one language first.

### Utility Filtering
- **Definition**: The process of screening potential learning goals using criteria like career impact, interest levels, and time availability to select the highest-value skill.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 5 as a practical way to choose your next learning focus.
- **Real-World Application**: Screen your study list and pick the skill (e.g., public speaking) that will most directly lead to a promotion at work.

### Skill Deconstruction
- **Definition**: Breaking down a complex, macro capability into its smallest fundamental parts (microcomponents) to identify which subskills to drill first.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 6 as a prerequisite to choosing study resources.
- **Real-World Application**: Deconstruct the skill of photography into: exposure settings, lighting, composition, and post-editing.

### Resource Limit Rule
- **Definition**: The constraint of limiting your study materials to exactly 5–6 high-quality books, courses, or guides to prevent information overload.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 6 as Step 3 to maintain focus and speed up execution.
- **Real-World Application**: Pick the top-rated textbook and course on database design, put all other recommendations in a bookmarks folder, and ignore them.

## 🛠️ Note-Taking, Projects, & Habits

### Action-Oriented Notes
- **Definition**: A note-taking methodology that structures notes around immediate, practical execution steps and questions rather than passive copy-transcripts.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 7 as Step 4 of the framework.
- **Real-World Application**: When reading about design patterns, write: "I will implement a Factory Pattern in my project tomorrow by doing X."

### Active Synthesis
- **Definition**: The cognitive act of translating source material into your own plain-language words, forcing you to actively process the meaning.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 7 as a way to build deep conceptual understanding.
- **Real-World Application**: Summarize an academic paper by writing a 2-sentence description from memory for a non-technical friend.

### Project-Based Consolidation
- **Definition**: The practice of designing a hands-on, time-bound project that requires the application of a skill to complete, cementing abstract theory.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 8 as Step 5 of the framework.
- **Real-World Application**: Learn carpentry by committing to build a small wooden bookshelf for your living room over the weekend.

### Done Definition
- **Definition**: The clear, objective criteria that determines when a project or study session is officially completed, preventing goal-post shifting.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 8 as an essential metric for project design.
- **Real-World Application**: Define your project's completion as: "A functional 3-page website hosted online with a working contact form."

### Daily Time Boxing
- **Definition**: Scheduling a fixed, non-negotiable block of time on your calendar daily (e.g., 30 minutes) dedicated strictly to deliberate practice.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 9 as Step 6 to automate study consistency.
- **Real-World Application**: Block off 7:30 AM to 8:00 AM every morning on your calendar for vocabulary study; treat it as an unchangeable meeting.
