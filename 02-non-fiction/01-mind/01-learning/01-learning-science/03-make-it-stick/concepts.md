# Key Concepts Glossary: Make It Stick

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

---

## Core Learning Strategies

### Retrieval Practice (The Testing Effect)

**Definition**: The act of actively pulling information from memory — without looking at source materials — rather than passively reviewing it. Also called the testing effect because being tested on material produces greater retention than additional study time.
**In the Book**: Central strategy of Chapter 2. Documented in the Roediger & Karpicke (2006) study where tested students retained 50% more after one week than students who reread the same material.
**Real-World Application**: After any lecture or reading session, close all materials and write or say aloud everything you can recall on a blank page before reviewing what you missed.

---

### Spaced Practice (The Spacing Effect)

**Definition**: Distributing study or practice sessions over time — with gaps between them — rather than concentrating all study into a single session. The spacing effect is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology, dating to Hermann Ebbinghaus (1885).
**In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 3 as the counterpart to (and replacement for) massed practice/cramming. The authors explain that "a little forgetting" between sessions makes subsequent retrieval more effortful — and therefore more consolidating.
**Real-World Application**: After covering a topic, schedule review sessions at 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, and 1 month — before you feel you need them.

---

### Interleaving

**Definition**: Mixing different types of problems, skills, or subjects within a single practice session rather than completing all of one type before moving to another (blocked practice).
**In the Book**: Chapter 3 introduces interleaving with the Kornell & Bjork (2008) art-style study, where interleaved learners dramatically outperformed blocked learners at identifying unseen paintings despite feeling they had learned less during practice.
**Real-World Application**: When studying mathematics, solve problems from three different chapters in one session rather than completing all problems from one chapter before moving to the next.

---

### Desirable Difficulties

**Definition**: Coined by Robert Bjork. Learning conditions that create short-term performance impairment (things feel harder, progress seems slower) but produce significantly more durable and transferable long-term retention.
**In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 4 as an umbrella concept covering generation, spacing, interleaving, variation, and elaboration. The key insight: if practice feels easy and fluid, it is likely ineffective for long-term retention.
**Real-World Application**: Deliberately introduce difficulty into your practice: vary problem types, attempt answers before reading solutions, change your study environment, mix old and new material together.

---

### Generation (Generation Effect / Pre-Testing)

**Definition**: Attempting to answer a question or solve a problem before being shown the answer or taught the solution. Even incorrect attempts prime the brain to encode the correct answer more deeply when encountered.
**In the Book**: Chapter 4 documents the generation effect: students pre-tested on material before a lecture retained more from the lecture than students who received no pre-test, even though the pre-tested group answered nearly every question incorrectly.
**Real-World Application**: Before reading a chapter, attempt to answer the end-of-chapter questions. Before a lecture, write down what you already know or can guess about the topic. The effort, not the accuracy, is what primes learning.

---

### Elaboration

**Definition**: The practice of explaining new material in your own words and explicitly connecting it to things you already know, by asking "why," "how," and "what does this remind me of?" questions.
**In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 4 as a desirable difficulty. The more connections you draw between new material and existing knowledge, the more retrieval pathways you create — making the memory accessible from more contextual triggers.
**Real-World Application**: After reading any new concept, ask yourself: Why does this make sense? How does this relate to [existing knowledge]? What would be a good analogy for this? Write the answers without looking at the text.

---

### Elaborative Interrogation

**Definition**: A specific elaboration technique: asking and answering "why is this true?" and "how does this work?" for each new fact or concept encountered.
**In the Book**: Referenced as a specific form of elaboration. Asking explanatory questions forces deep processing of incoming information rather than surface-level encoding.
**Real-World Application**: For each new fact you encounter, write one "why" question and one "how" question, then answer them without reference materials.

---

### Reflection

**Definition**: Deliberately reviewing what happened after an experience, lesson, or practice session — what went well, what did not, what would you do differently, and what principles or knowledge the experience reinforces.
**In the Book**: Chapter 4 presents reflection as a desirable difficulty because it forces retrieval (recalling what happened), elaboration (connecting it to principles), and generation (producing next-step insights). It converts implicit experience into explicit, transferable knowledge.
**Real-World Application**: After any study session, exam, or real-world application, write for 5 minutes answering: What did I learn? What confused me? What worked? What would I do differently? What concepts does this connect to?

## Memory and Cognition Concepts

### Massed Practice (Cramming)

**Definition**: Concentrating all study of a topic into a single intensive session, with little or no spacing between study and use. The most commonly practised — and most consistently debunked — study strategy.
**In the Book**: Chapter 3 characterizes massed practice as producing fluency (the material feels familiar) without retention (the knowledge fades rapidly). It is compared unfavorably to spaced and interleaved practice throughout the book.
**Real-World Application**: Recognize when you are cramming, and break the session into multiple shorter sessions spread over days, even if the total study time is identical.

---

### Illusion of Mastery (Fluency Illusion)

**Definition**: The cognitive error of mistaking the ease of reading, recognizing, or processing information for actual mastery of that information. A property of the material's familiarity, not of internal knowledge.
**In the Book**: Central theme of Chapter 1 and Chapter 5. The authors explain that rereading produces fluency — material feels familiar and flows smoothly — which the brain misinterprets as retention. Remove the text, and the "knowledge" disappears.
**Real-World Application**: Never trust how familiar material feels as a measure of whether you know it. The only valid measure is closed-book retrieval — if you cannot reproduce it without prompts, you do not yet know it.

---

### Calibration

**Definition**: The process of aligning your internal confidence about what you know with your objective performance on retrieval tasks. Accurate calibration means your confidence matches your actual recall ability.
**In the Book**: Chapter 5 explains that most students are systematically over-confident (poor calibration) because they use fluency-based study methods that feel productive but produce weak retention. Low-stakes self-testing is the primary tool for recalibrating.
**Real-World Application**: Before any test or retrieval session, estimate your score as a percentage. After completing it, compare. If you consistently overestimate, you are using ineffective study strategies. Use this gap as diagnostic feedback.

---

### Metacognition

**Definition**: Thinking about your own thinking — the ability to monitor, evaluate, and regulate your own learning processes. High metacognition means accurately knowing what you know, what you don't know, and which strategies are working.
**In the Book**: Chapter 5 positions metacognition as an essential skill that counteracts the fluency illusion and prevents students from wasting time on ineffective strategies while believing they are learning.
**Real-World Application**: At the end of each study session, ask: "What did I actually learn? What can I reproduce right now without notes? What is still fuzzy?" Then adjust your next session based on the honest answers.

---

### Mental Model

**Definition**: A structured internal representation that organizes interconnected concepts, facts, and procedures into a meaningful whole — a "map" of a domain of knowledge that guides reasoning and problem-solving.
**In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 6 as the alternative to learning-style matching. Instead of tailoring instruction to a perceptual preference, the goal of instruction is to help all learners build rich, connected mental models of the material.
**Real-World Application**: After mastering individual facts in a domain, explicitly draw or describe how they connect — create a concept map, a flowchart, or a written explanation of the whole domain structure. This is the process of building a mental model.

## Mindset and Ability Concepts

### Growth Mindset

**Definition**: Carol Dweck's term for the belief that intelligence, talent, and ability are malleable — that they can be developed through effort, strategy, and learning from failure — in contrast to the Fixed Mindset, which treats ability as an innate, unchangeable trait.
**In the Book**: Chapter 7 presents the growth mindset as neurologically accurate: the brain does physically change through learning. Students taught that intelligence is malleable show measurably improved academic performance and resilience.
**Real-World Application**: When you encounter something difficult, consciously reframe: "I can't do this *yet* — and the struggle I'm experiencing right now is the signal that my brain is building new connections."

---

### Neuroplasticity

**Definition**: The brain's capacity to form new neural connections, strengthen existing ones, thicken myelin sheaths around active pathways, and generate new neurons (neurogenesis) in the hippocampus in response to learning, experience, and exercise.
**In the Book**: Chapter 7 uses neuroplasticity as the scientific foundation for the growth mindset claim — intelligence is not fixed because the physical substrate of intelligence (the brain) is not fixed.
**Real-World Application**: Recognize that every time you struggle with and eventually succeed at a difficult problem, a measurable physical change has occurred in your brain. The effort was not wasted; it was construction.

---

### Deliberate Practice

**Definition**: K. Anders Ericsson's term for the specific form of practice that drives expertise development: working at the edge of current competence, with specific performance goals, immediate feedback from a coach or objective measure, and focused intention to improve specific weaknesses.
**In the Book**: Chapter 7 distinguishes deliberate practice from mere practice. Spending 10,000 hours doing something does not build expertise — 10,000 hours of deliberate practice targeting specific weaknesses, with immediate feedback, does.
**Real-World Application**: Identify your weakest skills within a domain. Practice those specifically — not the skills you already do well. Seek immediate, specific feedback after each practice attempt, and adjust your approach based on it.

---

### Grit

**Definition**: Angela Duckworth's term for long-term passion and persistence toward a challenging goal, even in the face of failure, boredom, and adversity. Grit predicts achievement more reliably than IQ alone in most domains.
**In the Book**: Chapter 7 presents grit alongside neuroplasticity and growth mindset as the three psychological multipliers that determine whether a person develops expertise over time.
**Real-World Application**: When you want to quit a difficult subject or project, distinguish between productive difficulty (you are learning) and unproductive difficulty (your strategy is wrong). Persist through the former; adapt through the latter.

---

### The Testing Effect

**Definition**: The empirically documented finding that being tested on material produces greater long-term retention than spending an equivalent amount of time restudying the same material.
**In the Book**: Chapter 2's central concept, supported by the landmark Roediger & Karpicke (2006) experiment. The testing effect has been replicated hundreds of times across diverse subjects, ages, and learning contexts.
**Real-World Application**: Replace your last rereading session before any exam with a practice test session — even if you feel underprepared. The act of testing yourself will produce more retention than another pass through the notes.

---

### The Feynman Technique

**Definition**: A calibration method named after physicist Richard Feynman: explain a concept in simple, plain language as if teaching it to someone who has never encountered it before. Wherever the explanation breaks down or becomes vague, that is precisely where your understanding is weakest.
**In the Book**: Referenced in Chapter 5 as a metacognitive tool for identifying gaps in understanding that fluency-based studying conceals.
**Real-World Application**: After studying any concept, close all materials and write a 1-page explanation of it addressed to a smart 12-year-old. Everywhere you find yourself using jargon you cannot define, or skipping over "obvious" steps, is a gap to return to.

## Study Strategies Summary Table

| Concept | What It Is | When to Use It |
|---|---|---|
| Retrieval Practice | Testing yourself without looking at materials | After every study session, and in place of rereading |
| Spaced Practice | Distributing sessions over time | For any material you need to retain longer than 48 hours |
| Interleaving | Mixing problem/topic types in one session | Every practice session — avoid blocked single-topic drilling |
| Generation | Attempting before being shown the answer | Before reading a chapter or attending a lecture |
| Elaboration | Connecting new to known; asking why/how | During and immediately after every reading session |
| Reflection | Reviewing what happened and extracting principles | End of every study session and after real-world application |
| Calibration | Objective self-testing to measure actual knowledge | Before and after every study session |
| Deliberate Practice | Targeting your weakest skills with immediate feedback | When developing any technical or complex skill |
