# How We Learn: The Surprising Truth About When, Where, and Why It Happens

## Metadata
* **Authors**: Benedict Carey
* **Published**: 2014
* **Edition**: 1st (Random House)
* **Genre**: Learning Science / Cognitive Psychology / Popular Science
* **Description**: A science-backed investigation into how the brain processes, stores, and retrieves information. The book challenges traditional study paradigms — such as dedicated quiet study areas, rigid routines, and cramming — and replaces them with evidence-based techniques like context variation, spaced learning, active recall, incubation, percolation, and sleep consolidation.
* **Companion Books**: *Make It Stick* (Brown et al.), *A Mind for Numbers* (Oakley), *Why Don't Students Like School?* (Willingham)

## Should You Read the Full Book?

**Read the full book if** you enjoy narrative non-fiction, storytelling, and the history of scientific discovery. Benedict Carey, a veteran science reporter for *The New York Times*, writes with a highly engaging, journalistic style. The book is filled with historical anecdotes of quirky researchers, personal stories of his own academic struggles, and clear, accessible explanations of complex neuroscience experiments (such as the context-dependency study involving scuba divers). If you want an entertaining, light, and narrative-rich entry point into the cognitive science of learning, the book is a delightful read.

**Use the summaries instead if** you have already read books like *Make It Stick* or *A Mind for Numbers* and simply want a quick checklist of actionable techniques. The core principles discussed here — spaced repetition, interleaving, testing, and sleep stages — are standard in learning science, and the specific additions of context variation and incubation are easily summarized. If you want a direct, fluff-free execution guide and want to bypass the journalistic backstories, historical framing, and personal anecdotes, these summaries will give you the complete toolset in a fraction of the time.

> **Verdict**: *Read the full book if you want a highly readable, story-driven, and engaging introduction to learning science. Use the summaries if you want to skip the narratives and get a direct, actionable blueprint of the cognitive strategies.*
