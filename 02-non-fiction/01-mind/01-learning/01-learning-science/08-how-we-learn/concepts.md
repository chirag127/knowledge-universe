# Key Concepts Glossary: How We Learn

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

---

## 🧠 Brain Architecture & Forgetting

### Brain as a Foraging System
- **Definition**: The evolutionary model of the brain as an organic, searching system designed to gather varied cues from dynamic environments, rather than a computer processor or a muscle.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in the Introduction as Carey’s central thesis, explaining why the brain prefers variation over static repetition.
- **Real-World Application**: Study a topic using multiple media (podcasts, code, articles) to align with your brain's natural tendency to forage for varied information.

### Entorhinal Filter
- **Definition**: The brain structure (specifically the entorhinal cortex) that serves as a sensory gatekeeper, filtering out incoming noise and determining what data reaches the hippocampus.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 1 during the overview of memory biology and the case of H.M.
- **Real-World Application**: Reduce cognitive overload by limiting random visual and auditory distractions, letting your entorhinal cortex focus on encoding study materials.

### Storage Strength
- **Definition**: The permanent depth and stability of a memory trace in the brain's long-term neocortex, which never decays once encoded.
- **In the Book**: Featured in Chapter 2 as a core component of Robert Bjork’s New Theory of Disuse.
- **Real-World Application**: Focus on active retrieval rather than passive reading to build high storage strength for critical programming syntax or vocabulary.

### Retrieval Strength
- **Definition**: The temporary accessibility of a memory at any given moment, which fluctuates based on cues and decays rapidly if not used.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 2, contrasting it with storage strength.
- **Real-World Application**: Acknowledge that forgetting a phone number you learned yesterday is a decay in retrieval strength, not a permanent loss of the data.

### Bjork's New Theory of Disuse
- **Definition**: A memory theory stating that storage strength is permanent, whereas retrieval strength decays over time; a decay in retrieval strength makes the next recall effort harder, boosting storage strength.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 2 to explain the psychological benefit of forgetting.
- **Real-World Application**: Space out your study sessions so you partially forget the material; the effort required to recall it will solidify the memory permanent-storage.

## 🗺️ Context & Spacing Dynamics

### Context-Dependent Memory
- **Definition**: The cognitive phenomenon where memory recall is strongest when the physical and emotional variables during retrieval match the context of encoding.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 3, referencing Godden & Baddeley’s 1975 study on scuba divers.
- **Real-World Application**: If you must perform under high stress (like a coding interview), practice under stress and in a professional office environment to align the context.

### Context Variation
- **Definition**: Intentionally changing study environments (e.g., room layout, background sounds, lighting) to detach the memory from any single environmental cue.
- **In the Book**: Recommended in Chapter 3 as a strategy to build flexible, environment-proof memories.
- **Real-World Application**: Study your coding course at a kitchen table one day, in a coffee shop the next, and at a library desk the day after.

### The Spacing Effect
- **Definition**: The learning principle where distributed review sessions over time yield significantly higher long-term retention than massed study (cramming).
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 4, tracing the concept back to Ebbinghaus and Bahrick's optimal spacing research.
- **Real-World Application**: Review your notes for 10 minutes every two days instead of studying them for 2 hours on Sunday night.

### The Fluency Illusion
- **Definition**: The cognitive bias where ease of reading or recognizing material is mistaken for actual recall ability and understanding.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 5 to explain why rereading highlighted textbooks is highly ineffective.
- **Real-World Application**: Close your study guide and explain a design pattern from memory to test if you actually know it, rather than just recognizing the text.

### The Pretest Effect
- **Definition**: The phenomenon where attempting to answer questions on new material before studying it primes the brain to encode the correct answers more deeply.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 5 as a powerful priming strategy that turns passive reading into active search.
- **Real-World Application**: Take a chapter quiz before reading the textbook pages, even if you guess on every single question.

## 💡 Problem Solving & Creativity

### Incubation
- **Definition**: The process of walking away from a problem after hitting a wall, allowing the subconscious mind to search memory networks for connections in the background.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 6 as a core strategy to break through cognitive fixation and solve difficult problems.
- **Real-World Application**: When stuck on a programming bug, take a 10-minute walk to let your mind incubate the problem rather than staring at the screen.

### Cognitive Fixation
- **Definition**: A mental state (also called the Einstellung Effect or functional fixedness) where a learner gets stuck on a wrong, rigid approach, blocking creative solutions.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 6 to explain why continuous grinding on a problem is often counterproductive.
- **Real-World Application**: Reset your approach to a project by stepping away and starting a new, blank document to avoid repeating past design errors.

### Percolation
- **Definition**: The background mental processing of a long-term, incomplete project, which keeps the brain alert to relevant cues in the environment.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 7, linking it to the Zeigarnik Effect and selective attention.
- **Real-World Application**: Draft a project outline on Monday so that your mind percolates it throughout the week, drawing inspiration from random articles or sights.

### The Zeigarnik Effect
- **Definition**: The psychological phenomenon where the brain remembers incomplete or interrupted tasks far better than completed ones.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 7 to explain why starting projects early and leaving them unfinished aids creativity.
- **Real-World Application**: Stop writing a report mid-sentence at the end of the day; it makes starting the next morning easier because your brain kept the sentence active overnight.

### Interleaving
- **Definition**: Mixing different subjects, concepts, or problem types in a single study session to teach the brain how to select the correct strategy.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 8 as the opposite of blocked practice, referencing sports and art studies.
- **Real-World Application**: Study math by mixing algebra, geometry, and calculus problems rather than doing 20 algebra problems in a row.

## 💤 Subconscious & Consolidation

### Perceptual Learning
- **Definition**: The process where exposure to varied visual or sensory stimuli trains the sensory cortex to automatically recognize patterns and make discriminations without conscious analysis.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 9, detailing how the visual system can build instant intuition.
- **Real-World Application**: Learn to read server logs by looking at dozens of healthy vs. crashed server charts, training your eyes to spot anomalies instantly.

### Perceptual Learning Modules (PLMs)
- **Definition**: Specialized, rapid-exposure training programs designed to build visual intuition by showing images requiring fast categorization with immediate feedback.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 9, explaining how PLMs are used to train pilots and medical diagnostics.
- **Real-World Application**: Use flashcard apps to rapidly classify different code design patterns (e.g., Singleton vs. Factory) to build programming intuition.

### Memory Consolidation
- **Definition**: The physical stabilization of memory traces in the neocortex, occurring primarily during the NREM and REM phases of sleep.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 10 as the core cognitive purpose of sleep.
- **Real-World Application**: Secure your study gains by ensuring you get a full 8 hours of sleep after a day of learning, rather than staying up to pull an all-nighter.

### Slow-Wave Sleep (Deep Sleep)
- **Definition**: The NREM sleep phase characterized by slow brain waves, occurring mostly in the first half of the night, which consolidates declarative memories (facts, dates, vocab).
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 10, highlighting its role in stabilizing semantic knowledge.
- **Real-World Application**: Prioritize early-night sleep if you spent the day memorizing terms or vocabulary words for an upcoming exam.

### REM Sleep
- **Definition**: The rapid eye movement sleep phase characterized by high brain activity, occurring mostly in the morning, which consolidates motor skills, spatial coordination, and creative associations.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 10, highlighting its role in consolidating music practice, athletic skills, and creative problem-solving.
- **Real-World Application**: Do not wake up early to cram if you are preparing for a performance or a coding trial; cutting morning REM sleep ruins motor and creative consolidation.
