# Key Concepts Glossary: Small Teaching

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

---

## ⚾ Core Philosophy & Knowledge

### Small Teaching
- **Definition**: An educational approach that focuses on making minor, research-based modifications to lectures, course designs, and communications to improve learning outcomes.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in the Introduction as the central framework of the book, contrasting with massive curriculum overhauls.
- **Real-World Application**: Optimize your study routine by adding a 5-minute quiz at the start of your day rather than completely redesigning your calendar.

### Small-Ball Metaphor
- **Definition**: The sports-derived philosophy that long-term success is built on the accumulation of minor, strategic, and low-risk plays (walks, steals, bunts) rather than high-risk home runs.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in the Introduction to explain why incremental changes are more sustainable and effective for teachers and students.
- **Real-World Application**: Focus on studying 20 minutes daily (small-ball) rather than pulling all-nighters before milestones (home runs).

### Predicting
- **Definition**: The active cognitive process of guessing the answer to a question or the outcome of a scenario before formal instruction is provided.
- **In the Book**: Featured in Chapter 1 as a key method in the Knowledge section to prime memory networks.
- **Real-World Application**: Pause at a new section header in a programming manual and guess what the code syntax will be before reading the text.

### Predictive Priming
- **Definition**: The neurological effect where attempting to answer questions on new material activates relevant prior knowledge, preparing the brain to receive and encode the correct answer.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 1 to explain why guessing is beneficial even when the guess is incorrect.
- **Real-World Application**: Take a quick pre-quiz at the start of a chapter to prime your attention before reading.

### Pre-testing
- **Definition**: An active learning strategy where a student takes an assessment on material before studying it to highlight key concepts and focus selective attention.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 1 as a structured way to implement prediction.
- **Real-World Application**: Attempt a mock certification exam before starting the training course to audit your current background baseline.

## 🔄 Cognitive Strategies & Connections

### Retrieving
- **Definition**: The cognitive act of pulling information out of long-term memory, which strengthens neural pathways and alters the memory trace for future recall.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 2 as the second core strategy of the Knowledge section.
- **Real-World Application**: Close your book and list the three main concepts you just read about from memory.

### Active Recall
- **Definition**: The study strategy of testing oneself by generating answers from memory rather than passively reviewing notes or textbooks.
- **In the Book**: Contrasted with passive rereading in Chapter 2 to show why cognitive effort is necessary for retention.
- **Real-World Application**: Convert your study notes into flashcards with questions on one side and answers on the other.

### Interleaving
- **Definition**: Mixing different subjects, concepts, or problem types in a single study session to teach the brain how to select the correct strategy.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 3 as the third core strategy of the Knowledge section.
- **Real-World Application**: Study math by mixing algebra, geometry, and calculus problems rather than doing 20 algebra problems in a row.

### Strategy Discrimination
- **Definition**: The cognitive skill of determining which formula, concept, or technique applies to a specific problem when faced with a mixed scenario.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 3 as the primary cognitive benefit of interleaved practice over blocked practice.
- **Real-World Application**: Practice mixing mock test questions from different chapters to learn how to identify which formula to use on exams.

### Connecting
- **Definition**: The cognitive process of actively linking new information to existing facts, personal memories, or broader conceptual frameworks.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 4 as the first strategy of the Understanding section.
- **Real-World Application**: When learning a new programming language, connect its loop syntax to the loop structure of a language you already know.

## 🛠️ Deep Understanding & Practice

### Elaboration
- **Definition**: A learning strategy that involves explaining and describing new concepts in detail, connecting them to prior knowledge, and asking explanatory "why" questions.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 4 as the mechanism that converts raw facts into networks of understanding.
- **Real-World Application**: Write a paragraph explaining *why* a database index speeds up search queries, connecting it to a book's index.

### Concept Mapping
- **Definition**: A visual representation of knowledge showing the connections, relationships, and hierarchies between different concepts.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 4 as a practical, small-teaching exercise to build connections.
- **Real-World Application**: Draw a quick diagram linking "class," "object," "inheritance," and "polymorphism" in object-oriented programming.

### Cognitive Fidelity
- **Definition**: The alignment between the cognitive demands of a practice task and the cognitive demands of the target performance or assessment.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 5 to show why practice must match the format of the exam.
- **Real-World Application**: If your exam is essay-based, practice drafting arguments and writing paragraphs rather than doing multiple-choice quizzes.

### Self-Explanation
- **Definition**: The cognitive act of explaining the steps, logic, and reasoning of a problem or concept to yourself to clarify understanding and expose logical gaps.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 6 as a core method to build deep comprehension.
- **Real-World Application**: Walk through a line of code and explain to yourself exactly what each command is doing to debug a problem.

### Peer Teaching
- **Definition**: Explaining concepts to a study partner or colleague, which tests and deepens comprehension for both parties by requiring simplification.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 6 as a collaborative, small-teaching activity.
- **Real-World Application**: Teach a junior team member how to configure a server to verify your own understanding of the steps.

## 🌟 Motivation & Metacognition

### Social Belonging
- **Definition**: The psychological sense of acceptance, security, and identity within a learning group, which frees up cognitive resources for focus and memory.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 7 as the first step of the Inspiration section.
- **Real-World Application**: Join a study group or online community where mistakes are welcomed, reducing the anxiety of learning in isolation.

### Asset-Based Pedagogy
- **Definition**: A teaching approach that focuses on leveraging and expanding a learner's existing cultural and cognitive strengths rather than focusing on deficits.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 7 as the foundation of a supportive classroom environment.
- **Real-World Application**: Reframe your learning goals around what you already do well, using your strengths (e.g., visual organization) to learn new topics.

### Intrinsic Motivation
- **Definition**: The internal drive to engage in an activity because it is inherently interesting, satisfying, or personally meaningful, rather than for external rewards.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 8 as the key to long-term student engagement.
- **Real-World Application**: Learn a skill by connecting it to a personal passion project (e.g., learning coding to build your own personal website).

### Exam Wrapper
- **Definition**: A structured self-reflection tool used after an assessment to help learners analyze their study strategies, identify errors, and adjust their future habits.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 9 as a practical metacognitive small-teaching tool.
- **Real-World Application**: After receiving a project grade, write down: what study methods worked, what failed, and what you will do differently next time.
