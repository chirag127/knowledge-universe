# Key Concepts Glossary: Powerful Teaching

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

---

## 🏋️ Core Cognitive Frameworks

### Power Tools
- **Definition**: The four evidence-based cognitive strategies — retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, and feedback-driven metacognition — that boost student retention and performance.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 1 as the central framework of the book, representing simple, prep-free classroom tools.
- **Real-World Application**: Apply these tools to your own study schedule by combining flashcards (retrieval) with spaced, mixed study sessions.

### Three Stages of Learning
- **Definition**: The biological steps of memory: Encoding (getting info in), Storage (keeping info stored), and Retrieval (getting info out).
- **In the Book**: Described in Chapter 1 and 2 to explain why retrieval is the most critical phase for long-term memory.
- **Real-World Application**: Shift your study time from 90% reading (encoding) to 50% active recall testing (retrieval) to secure permanent storage.

### Putting In vs. Pulling Out
- **Definition**: The distinction between passive encoding (putting information in via lectures or reading) and active retrieval (pulling information out of memory).
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 2 as the primary shift required to transform learning from passive to active.
- **Real-World Application**: Close your book and explain a concept aloud (pulling out) instead of reading the chapter summary a second time (putting in).

### Retrieval Practice
- **Definition**: The cognitive act of pulling information out of long-term memory, which strengthens neural pathways and alters the memory trace for future recall.
- **In the Book**: The primary "Power Tool" discussed in Chapters 2 and 3 as the foundation of successful learning.
- **Real-World Application**: Test yourself on key terms using flashcards before looking at the definitions.

## 📝 Retrieval Tactics & Routines

### Brain Dump
- **Definition**: A free-recall strategy where a learner writes down everything they can remember about a topic on a blank page without referencing any notes.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 2 as a quick, low-prep retrieval warm-up activity.
- **Real-World Application**: After completing a study session, take 3 minutes to write down a raw list of all key terms you remember.

### Two Things
- **Definition**: A quick retrieval prompt asking students to list two specific takeaways from a previous lesson, reading, or slide.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 2 as a simple retrieval strategy that requires zero preparation or grading.
- **Real-World Application**: Pause halfway through an instructional video and write down "two things" explained in the first half.

### Retrieval Warm-Up
- **Definition**: Starting a study session with 3-4 questions about previous material before introducing new content to prime retrieval pathways.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 3 as a daily routine to transition students into class.
- **Real-World Application**: Begin your study day by answering a few quiz questions from yesterday's chapter before starting today's reading.

### Exit Ticket
- **Definition**: A quick retrieval prompt at the very end of a study session or class to capture the core takeaway or compile questions.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 3 as a routine exit check for student comprehension.
- **Real-World Application**: Write down the single most important lesson from your study block on a sticky note before closing your laptop.

### Failed Retrieval Benefit
- **Definition**: The cognitive reality that the mental struggle to retrieve information — even if the attempt is incorrect — primes the brain to encode the correct answer when it is shown.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 3 to encourage students to attempt answers rather than giving up quickly.
- **Real-World Application**: Force yourself to guess the answer to a practice question before clicking the "show answer" button, even if you are completely unsure.

## 📅 Spacing & Interleaving

### Spacing
- **Definition**: Distributing review and retrieval sessions over time (days or weeks) to allow partial forgetting to occur, which makes the next retrieval effortful and effective.
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 4 as the second core Power Tool.
- **Real-World Application**: Review vocabulary flashcards after 1 day, then 3 days, then 1 week, rather than drilling them for 3 hours in one night.

### Interleaving
- **Definition**: Mixing different types of problems, topics, or skills in a single practice session, rather than grouping them in blocks.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 4 as the third core Power Tool.
- **Real-World Application**: Study math by mixing algebra, geometry, and calculus problems rather than doing 20 algebra problems in a row.

### Blocked Practice
- **Definition**: Practicing one specific skill or problem type repeatedly before moving to another, which builds temporary familiarity but low long-term retention.
- **In the Book**: Contrasted with interleaving in Chapter 4 as an ineffective study habit.
- **Real-World Application**: Avoid doing 30 practice loops of the exact same coding function; mix in other algorithms to test your flexibility.

### Strategy Discrimination
- **Definition**: The cognitive skill of determining which formula, concept, or technique applies to a specific problem when faced with a mixed scenario.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 4 as the primary cognitive benefit of interleaved practice.
- **Real-World Application**: Practice mixing mock test questions from different chapters to learn how to identify which formula to use on exams.

## 🎯 Metacognition & Environment

### Metacognition
- **Definition**: The ability to analyze, monitor, and assess your own cognitive states and knowledge gaps (knowing what you know vs. what you do not know).
- **In the Book**: Introduced in Chapter 5 as the fourth core Power Tool.
- **Real-World Application**: Write down what concepts you feel weak in, then take a practice test to see if your self-assessment matches reality.

### Metacognitive Calibration
- **Definition**: The process of aligning your internal confidence with your actual performance, using objective testing to remove the fluency illusion.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 5 as a key step in helping students avoid overestimating their preparation.
- **Real-World Application**: Before checking your test score, estimate what score you think you got; track your predictions to calibrate your confidence.

### Fluency Illusion
- **Definition**: The cognitive bias where ease of reading, recognizing, or looking at materials is mistaken for actual recall ability and understanding.
- **In the Book**: Discussed in Chapter 5 to explain why students who reread notes are often shocked by low exam scores.
- **Real-World Application**: Do not assume you know a concept just because you read it in a book and thought "that makes sense"; test yourself without the book to be sure.

### Low-Stakes Environment
- **Definition**: A supportive practice space where errors and retrieval attempts do not carry heavy academic, grading, or personal penalties.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 8 as the necessary condition to reduce student anxiety during retrieval practice.
- **Real-World Application**: Keep your daily self-tests ungraded and casual to normalize the process of recall and prevent anxiety from clogging working memory.

### Test Desensitization
- **Definition**: The reduction of exam-day anxiety achieved by performing low-stakes, ungraded retrieval practice daily.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 8 as a way to prepare students mentally for high-stakes standardized tests.
- **Real-World Application**: Treat daily quiz practice as a routine habit to desensitize your mind to the stress of recall.

### Metacognitive Buy-In
- **Definition**: Explaining the science of learning directly to learners to motivate them to use difficult but effective study strategies like retrieval and spacing.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 9 as a way to overcome student resistance to the struggle of active recall.
- **Real-World Application**: Remind yourself that when study feels difficult, that is a sign of long-term consolidation happening in your brain, not a sign of failure.
