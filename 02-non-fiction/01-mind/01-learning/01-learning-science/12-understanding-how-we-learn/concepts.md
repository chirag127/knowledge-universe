# Key Concepts Glossary: Understanding How We Learn: A Visual Guide

All key terms, mental models, frameworks, and named concepts introduced in this book — defined in plain language.

## 🔬 Scientific Foundations & Processes

### Evidence-Based Education
- **Definition**: The practice of selecting educational methods and study strategies based on rigorous, replicated empirical research rather than personal intuition or tradition.
- **In the Book**: Explored in Chapter 1 and 2 as the necessary alternative to the subjective "curse of knowledge" and teaching myths.
- **Real-World Application**: When building a study schedule, choose spaced retrieval practice (evidence-backed) over highlight rereading (intuition-backed).

### Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)
- **Definition**: An experimental study design where subjects are randomly assigned to either a treatment group (using a strategy) or a control group (using a placebo/standard) to measure causal effects.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 2 as the scientific gold standard for verifying the effectiveness of educational techniques.
- **Real-World Application**: Trust a learning study's conclusions only if it compared group scores using randomized controls rather than just surveying a single class.

### Curse of Knowledge
- **Definition**: The cognitive bias where experts find it extremely difficult to think about a topic from a novice's perspective because they have automated the background steps.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 3 to explain why experts struggle to teach beginners without clear scaffolding.
- **Real-World Application**: When writing instructions for a junior developer, explain terms like "Git rebase" rather than assuming they understand the terminal commands.

### Fluency Illusion
- **Definition**: The cognitive bias where the ease of reading or recognizing material is mistaken for actual recall ability and long-term understanding.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 3 as the primary trap that leads students to choose passive, ineffective study methods.
- **Real-World Application**: Avoid overconfidence by closing your textbook and writing down the key steps of a process from memory, rather than just reading it.

### Neuromyth
- **Definition**: A commonly held, unscientific belief about brain function and learning that lacks empirical support (e.g., learning styles).
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 4, outlining how myths arise from commercial interests and intuitive appeal.
- **Real-World Application**: Stop separating learning resources into visual/auditory styles; all students learn best when the media matches the content meaning.

## 🧠 Cognitive Architecture Bottlenecks

### Sensory Overload
- **Definition**: Cognitive exhaustion and decrease in learning efficiency caused by presenting too many competing visual, auditory, or sensory inputs simultaneously.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 5 (Perception) to warn against cluttered, text-heavy slide designs.
- **Real-World Application**: Study in a clean, uncluttered workspace and use minimalist slides to prevent your eyes from getting distracted by non-essential details.

### Attention Bottleneck
- **Definition**: The biological limitation of working memory that prevents the brain from consciously focusing on more than one cognitively demanding task at a time.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 6 as the gateway to encoding, making divided attention fatal to learning.
- **Real-World Application**: Put your phone in another room while studying to ensure your working memory is dedicated 100% to the subject.

### Task-Switching Cost
- **Definition**: The loss of cognitive speed, accuracy, and depth caused by constantly shifting focus back and forth between two tasks (e.g., texting while studying).
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 6 to explain why the popular concept of multitasking is a cognitive impossibility.
- **Real-World Application**: Block all notifications during your study time; checking one message drains your working memory and slows down learning.

### Memory Encoding
- **Definition**: The cognitive process of translating fragile inputs from working memory into physical neural connections in long-term memory.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 7 as the bridge that connects attention with long-term retention.
- **Real-World Application**: Reinforce encoding by immediately writing a summary or explaining the concept aloud after reading.

## 📋 The Six Core Learning Strategies

### Spaced Practice
- **Definition**: Distributing study and review sessions over time (days or weeks) rather than massing them into a single marathon session.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 8 as the first core strategy for planning study calendars.
- **Real-World Application**: Review vocabulary flashcards for 10 minutes every day instead of cramming them for 2 hours on Sunday.

### Interleaving
- **Definition**: Mixing different subjects, concepts, or problem types in a single study session to teach the brain how to select the correct strategy.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 8 as the second core planning strategy.
- **Real-World Application**: Study math by mixing algebra, geometry, and calculus problems rather than doing 20 algebra problems in a row.

### Strategy Discrimination
- **Definition**: The cognitive skill of determining which formula, concept, or technique applies to a specific problem when faced with a mixed scenario.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 8 as the primary cognitive benefit of interleaved practice over blocked practice.
- **Real-World Application**: Practice mixing mock test questions from different chapters to learn how to identify which formula to use on exams.

### Elaboration
- **Definition**: A learning strategy that involves explaining and describing new concepts in detail, connecting them to prior knowledge, and asking explanatory "why" questions.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 9 as the first strategy for developing deep understanding.
- **Real-World Application**: Ask yourself: "Why does this algorithm work this way? How does it connect to memory allocation?"

### Concrete Examples
- **Definition**: Using specific, real-world cases, scenarios, or analogies to ground and illustrate abstract concepts, helping novices perceive deep structures.
- **In the Book**: Covered in Chapter 9 as the second strategy for developing understanding.
- **Real-World Application**: Learn the abstract concept of "object-oriented inheritance" by looking at a concrete analogy like a parent and child.

### Retrieval Practice
- **Definition**: The cognitive act of recalling information from memory without looking at notes, altering the memory trace for future recall.
- **In the Book**: Detailed in Chapter 10 as the primary strategy to reinforce learning.
- **Real-World Application**: Write down a summary of a chapter from memory on a blank sheet of paper before checking your notes.

### Dual Coding
- **Definition**: Combining verbal descriptions with visual representations (diagrams, timelines, maps) to create two separate neural pathways to the same memory.
- **In the Book**: Highlighted in Chapter 10 as the second strategy to reinforce memory.
- **Real-World Application**: When memorizing a process, write down the steps *and* sketch a simple flowchart showing the sequence.

### Visual Scaffolding
- **Definition**: Using simple drawings, icons, or visual flowcharts to support and organize abstract text, making the relations between concepts explicit.
- **In the Book**: Pioneered visually by designer Oliver Caviglioli throughout the book's layouts.
- **Real-World Application**: Use flowcharts or UML diagrams to visualize the relationships between database tables in software.

## ⚡ Application & Routines

### Lagged Homework
- **Definition**: Assigning homework tasks on concepts learned weeks ago to force spaced retrieval and prevent the fading of older skills.
- **In the Book**: Recommended in Chapter 11 as an evidence-based tip for teachers to structure homework.
- **Real-World Application**: When designing a personal study plan, allocate 20% of your weekly homework time to solving problems from unit 1.

### Distraction Management
- **Definition**: The active removal of physical and digital sensory triggers (e.g., locking phones, quiet zones) to protect working memory capacity from task-switching.
- **In the Book**: Outlined in Chapter 12 as an essential tip for student success.
- **Real-World Application**: Use website blocker apps to restrict access to social media during your scheduled study blocks.
