# A Mind for Numbers: How to Excel at Math and Science (Even If You Flunked Algebra)

## Metadata
* **Author**: Barbara Oakley, PhD
* **Published**: 2014
* **Edition**: 1st (TarcherPerigee / Penguin Random House)
* **Genre**: Learning Science / Cognitive Psychology / STEM Education
* **Description**: Barbara Oakley translates decades of neuroscience and cognitive psychology research into a practical guide for excelling in mathematics, science, and any technically demanding subject. Built around the brain's two fundamental thinking modes — Focused and Diffuse — it teaches chunking, memory techniques, procrastination defeat, and test-taking strategy for learners who believe they "aren't math people."
* **Companion Books**: *Learning How to Learn* (Oakley, Sejnowski, McConville — teen adaptation), *Make It Stick* (Brown et al.), *Peak* (Ericsson)

## Should You Read the Full Book?

**Read the full book if** you are a student, career-changer, or any adult who believes they "can't do math or science" and needs both the science and the personal transformation narrative. The book's 18 chapters are structured as a progressive course — each chapter has a "Pause and Recall" section and exercises that only work if you engage with them while reading. The stories of Oakley's own transformation and the Santiago Ramón y Cajal case study are core to the book's motivational impact and are not replaceable by summaries.

**Use the summaries instead if** you have already read *Learning How to Learn* (the teen adaptation of this same material), completed Oakley's free Coursera course, or are primarily interested in the frameworks (Focused/Diffuse modes, Chunking, Zombie habits, Pomodoro) as reference material. The conceptual content of this book and *Learning How to Learn* overlap significantly — if you have done one, the summaries here capture the incremental value of the other.

> **Verdict**: *Struggling with a hard subject and need a full mindset shift → read the full book. Already familiar with Oakley's frameworks from any source → the summaries here are sufficient.*
