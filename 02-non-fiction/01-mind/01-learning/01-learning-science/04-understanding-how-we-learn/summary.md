**Verdict**: The most visually accessible and teacher-friendly
introduction to cognitive learning strategies available. If you have
already read *Make It Stick*, the overlap is substantial (~70%), but the
illustrations, Part 1 (evidence literacy), and Part 4 (role-specific tips)
add unique value. For newcomers to learning science, this is the ideal
entry point — shorter, clearer, more practical than its peers.

## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Understanding How We Learn bridges a dangerous gap: educational practice
rarely relies on research findings, defaulting instead to intuition,
tradition, and marketing fads. Cognitive psychologists Yana Weinstein and
Megan Sumeracki (founders of The Learning Scientists) equip educators,
students, and parents with the tools to evaluate evidence critically and
apply six scientifically validated learning strategies — spaced practice,
interleaving, elaboration, concrete examples, dual coding, and retrieval
practice. The book systematically dismantles persistent myths (learning
styles, left-brain/right-brain, multitasking) while building a
neurologically grounded understanding of perception, attention, and
memory. Oliver Caviglioli's illustrations are not decorative; they embody
the dual-coding principle the book advocates, making complex cognitive
science instantly graspable. The core message: learning that feels easy
usually isn't working, and effortful strategies — though initially
uncomfortable — produce durable, transferable knowledge.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### Pillar 1: Evidence Literacy — Knowing Good Science from Bad
**Cross-ref: Ch 1–4**

- Education suffers a communication breakdown with cognitive science:
teachers lack access, time, and training to evaluate research
- Randomized controlled trials are the gold standard; correlational studies
show relationships but not causation
- Intuition is systematically unreliable — confirmation bias,
overconfidence, and "what feels right" often lead astray
- Pervasive misunderstandings (learning styles, brain gym, left/right
hemisphere myths) persist because refutational teaching is rare and grains
of truth make myths sticky
- Refutational teaching: present truth → name the misconception → refute
it with evidence → inoculate against future reinforcement

### Pillar 2: Cognitive Architecture — Perception, Attention, Memory
**Cross-ref: Ch 5–7**

- Perception is subjective: top-down processing (prior knowledge) shapes
how we interpret sensory information more than bottom-up raw sensation
- The "curse of knowledge" — once you know something, you cannot easily
imagine not knowing it — creates systematic teaching blindspots
- Attention is a limited-capacity resource; true multitasking is
impossible (rapid task-switching is the reality, with cognitive switching
costs)
- Cognitive Load Theory: working memory (3–5 slots) is easily
overwhelmed; instructional design must minimise extraneous load
- Memory is reconstructive, not archival — every retrieval modifies the
original trace, making memory fallible but also improvable through
practice

### Pillar 3: Strategy 1 — Spaced Practice & Interleaving
**Cross-ref: Ch 8**

- Spacing study sessions over time (distributed practice) dramatically
outperforms massed practice (cramming) for long-term retention
- First studied by Ebbinghaus (1885); replicated across age groups,
subjects, and contexts for 130+ years
- Interleaving: mixing different topics or problem types during practice.
Feels harder initially (good!), produces superior long-term discrimination
and transfer
- Blocked practice creates an illusion of fluency that collapses under
delayed testing; interleaving builds robust, flexible knowledge

### Pillar 4: Strategy 2 — Elaboration & Concrete Examples
**Cross-ref: Ch 9**

- Elaborative interrogation: asking "how" and "why" questions about new
material forces deep processing and integration with prior knowledge
- Self-explanation: articulating reasoning processes while solving
problems improves performance on subsequent tests
- Concrete examples ground abstract concepts; multiple varied examples
prevent "overfitting" to surface features
- Dual coding: combining words with pictures exploits separate processing
channels (verbal + visual), improving encoding and retrieval

### Pillar 5: Strategy 3 — Retrieval Practice (The Testing Effect)
**Cross-ref: Ch 10**

- Actively recalling information from memory strengthens the memory trace
(reconsolidation). Each retrieval makes the memory more durable and
accessible
- Low-stakes, frequent quizzing is the most practical classroom
implementation
- Retrieval practice provides indirect benefits too: feedback on what
students actually know (vs. what they think they know)
- The effect is strongest when retrieval is spaced after a delay
(immediate retrieval favours short-term recall; delayed retrieval builds
long-term retention)
- Scaffolding is important: too-difficult retrieval demotivates; feedback
(corrective) is essential

### Pillar 6: Practical Application — Role-Specific Implementation
**Cross-ref: Ch 11–13**

- Teachers: lagged homework, cumulative quizzes, WOOP goal-setting,
cognitive load management in presentations
- Students: create study schedules early, use practice tests, combine all
six strategies, prefer longhand notes over digital, sleep well
- Parents: establish spaced homework routines, encourage elaboration
through "how does this relate to real life?" questions, model good study
habits, avoid rewards that undermine intrinsic motivation

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

### Spaced Practice
- **Definition**: Distributing learning sessions over time rather than
concentrating them in a single massed session
- **In the Book**: The single strongest contribution of cognitive
psychology to education; benefits require a delay between study and test
- **Real-World Application**: Review material on day 1, day 3, day 7, day
14; assign lagged homework that revisits old material

### Interleaving
- **Definition**: Mixing different topics, problem types, or skills during
practice rather than blocking them by type
- **In the Book**: Feels harder and produces lower initial performance,
but leads to superior long-term retention and transfer
- **Real-World Application**: When studying math, alternate between
algebra, geometry, and statistics problems in one session

### Elaborative Interrogation
- **Definition**: A self-questioning technique where learners ask "why"
and "how" about new information
- **In the Book**: Forces integration of new knowledge with existing
mental models; produces deeper, more durable understanding
- **Real-World Application**: After reading a paragraph, stop and ask:
"Why does this make sense?" or "How does this connect to what I already
know?"

### Self-Explanation
- **Definition**: Articulating one's own reasoning process while
learning or solving problems
- **In the Book**: Especially effective in STEM contexts; improves not
just performance but understanding of underlying principles
- **Real-World Application**: While solving a physics problem, explain
each step aloud: "Now I'm applying conservation of momentum because..."

### Concrete Examples
- **Definition**: Specific, real-world instances that illustrate an
abstract concept
- **In the Book**: Multiple varied examples prevent students from
overfitting to surface features; the abstraction must be explicitly
connected
- **Real-World Application**: Teach "scarcity" via airline pricing +
sports tickets + water shortages, then explicitly state the common
principle

### Dual Coding
- **Definition**: Presenting information simultaneously through verbal and
visual channels
- **In the Book**: Words + pictures outperform words alone; visuals should
be relevant, not decorative, to avoid extraneous cognitive load
- **Real-World Application**: Create annotated diagrams; use sketchnoting
instead of linear text notes; replace bullet lists with concept maps

### Retrieval Practice
- **Definition**: Actively bringing information to mind from long-term
memory without reference materials
- **In the Book**: The "testing effect" — effortful retrieval strengthens
memory more than re-exposure. Feedback is essential for correcting errors
- **Real-World Application**: Close the book and write all you remember;
use practice tests; create flashcards and recall before flipping

### Cognitive Load
- **Definition**: The total amount of mental effort being used in working
memory
- **In the Book**: Three types — intrinsic (inherent complexity),
extraneous (poor design), germane (schema construction). Instruction
should minimise extraneous, manage intrinsic, optimise germane
- **Real-World Application**: Break complex procedures into steps; avoid
decorative clipart; use worked examples before independent practice

### Illusion of Competence
- **Definition**: Mistaking familiarity (from re-reading, highlighting)
for actual mastery
- **In the Book**: The most dangerous trap in student learning; retrieval
practice is the antidote because it reveals what you truly cannot recall
- **Real-World Application**: Never re-read without self-testing
afterward; if you can't explain it from memory, you don't know it

### Curse of Knowledge
- **Definition**: Experts' inability to imagine what it is like to not
know something
- **In the Book**: Systematically impedes teaching; teachers assume
students share their background understanding
- **Real-World Application**: Before explaining a concept, identify 3
assumptions you are making about your audience; test them explicitly

### Refutational Teaching
- **Definition**: A four-step method to correct misconceptions: teach the
truth, name the misconception, refute it with evidence, inoculate against
future arguments
- **In the Book**: More effective than simply presenting correct
information; repeatedly restating the misconception (even to refute it)
can backfire
- **Real-World Application**: "You may have heard that people have
learning styles. Research shows that matching instruction to preferred
styles does NOT improve learning. Here is why the studies fail..."

### Memory Reconsolidation
- **Definition**: The process by which retrieved memories become
temporarily malleable and are re-stored, potentially altered, each time
they are recalled
- **In the Book**: Explains both the fragility and the plasticity of
memory — why retrieval practice strengthens memory, and why false
memories can be implanted
- **Real-World Application**: Quizzing not only tests but transforms
memory; repeated accurate retrieval builds stronger, more accurate traces
