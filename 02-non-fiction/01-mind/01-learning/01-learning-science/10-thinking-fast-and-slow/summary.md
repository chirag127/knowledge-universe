## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

The human mind operates through two distinct cognitive systems: System 1, which is fast, automatic, intuitive, and emotional, and System 2, which is slow, effortful, deliberate, and logical. System 1 continuously generates impressions, feelings, and judgments that System 2 typically endorses with minimal scrutiny—a phenomenon Kahneman calls the "lazy controller." This arrangement is efficient for routine decisions but systematically produces predictable errors in judgment, known as cognitive biases, including anchoring, availability, representativeness, overconfidence, and loss aversion. The book is organized around Kahneman's decades of research (much of it with Amos Tversky) spanning judgment under uncertainty, heuristics and biases, prospect theory (which earned him the Nobel Prize in Economics), and the distinction between the experiencing self and the remembering self. Together, these ideas challenge the rational-agent model of classical economics and reveal that human decision-making is far more influenced by context, emotion, and cognitive shortcuts than most people realize.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

### 1. System 1
- **Definition**: The automatic, fast, intuitive mode of thinking that operates with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control.
- **In the Book**: The "protagonist" of most of the book's effects. It generates impressions, feelings, and inclinations that System 2 typically endorses. It jumps to conclusions, is biased toward belief, and is highly susceptible to priming and framing.
- **Real-World Application**: Recognizing a friend's face, reading emotions in someone's voice, driving on an empty road, answering 2+2.
- **Related Concepts**: Automatic processing, intuitive judgment, implicit cognition.

### 2. System 2
- **Definition**: The deliberate, analytical, effortful mode of thinking that requires conscious attention and is associated with the subjective experience of agency.
- **In the Book**: The "lazy controller." It monitors System 1's outputs and can override them—but usually doesn't because it requires effort. It is associated with "cognitive strain" and its capacity is limited (any diversion of attention degrades its performance).
- **Real-World Application**: Solving 17×24, parking in a tight space, checking the validity of a complex argument.
- **Related Concepts**: Controlled processing, executive function, working memory.

### 3. WYSIATI (What You See Is All There Is)
- **Definition**: System 1's tendency to construct the best possible story from only the information currently available, without considering what might be missing.
- **In the Book**: A foundational concept. WYSIATI explains why framing works, why we jump to conclusions, why we overestimate our understanding, and why we are so vulnerable to confirmation bias. It is the single most important mechanism behind cognitive biases in the book.
- **Real-World Application**: Reading a positive news article about a company's stock and assuming it's a good investment—without knowing what the article failed to report.
- **Related Concepts**: Bounded rationality, confirmation bias, framing effects, narrative fallacy.

### 4. Anchoring Effect
- **Definition**: The disproportionate influence of an initial value (the "anchor") on subsequent judgments. Different starting points yield different estimates, which are biased toward the initial value.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 11. Two mechanisms: anchoring as priming (System 1, automatic) and anchoring as insufficient adjustment (System 2, deliberate). Both Kahneman and Tversky were correct about different mechanisms.
- **Real-World Application**: Real estate agents setting a listing price that anchors buyer expectations; salary negotiations where the first offer anchors the discussion.
- **Related Concepts**: Priming, adjustment, suggestion effect.

### 5. Availability Heuristic
- **Definition**: Judging the frequency or probability of an event by the ease with which instances come to mind.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 12. Vivid, recent, or emotionally charged events are more available and therefore judged more probable. This is a System 1 shortcut that systematically distorts risk perception.
- **Real-World Application**: Overestimating the risk of plane crashes (because they're vividly reported) vs. car accidents; thinking more people die from shark attacks than from falling vending machines.
- **Related Concepts**: Salience bias, vividness effect, availability cascade.

### 6. Representativeness Heuristic
- **Definition**: Judging the probability that A belongs to category B by how similar A is to the stereotype of B, ignoring base rates and statistical evidence.
- **In the Book**: Chapters 14-15. The source of the conjunction fallacy (Linda problem). People prefer plausible, coherent stories over probabilistic reasoning.
- **Real-World Application**: Assuming a quiet, organized person who likes math is an engineer rather than a librarian, ignoring that librarians are more numerous in absolute terms.
- **Related Concepts**: Base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, stereotyping.

### 7. Prospect Theory
- **Definition**: A descriptive theory of decision under risk that replaces expected utility theory. Three core features: (1) evaluation relative to a reference point, (2) diminishing sensitivity to gains and losses (concave for gains, convex for losses), (3) loss aversion (losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains please).
- **In the Book**: Chapter 26. The centerpiece of Part IV and the work that won Kahneman the Nobel Prize. It explains risk aversion in gains, risk seeking in losses, and the endowment effect.
- **Real-World Application**: Investors hold losing stocks too long (risk-seeking in losses) and sell winning stocks too early (risk aversion in gains).
- **Related Concepts**: Expected utility theory, loss aversion, endowment effect, S-shaped value function.

### 8. Loss Aversion
- **Definition**: The tendency for losses to have a larger psychological impact than equivalent gains. The ratio is approximately 2:1 — losing $100 hurts about twice as much as gaining $100 pleases.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 27. Loss aversion explains the endowment effect (people value what they own more than what they don't), status quo bias, and the asymmetry in reactions to good and bad events.
- **Real-World Application**: People demand much more to sell an item they own than they would pay to buy it; companies are more motivated to avoid losses than to pursue gains.
- **Related Concepts**: Endowment effect, status quo bias, risk aversion, S-shaped value function.

### 9. The Endowment Effect
- **Definition**: The tendency for people to value something more simply because they own it. Selling prices are typically much higher than buying prices.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 27. A direct consequence of loss aversion: giving up an owned good is coded as a loss; acquiring it was coded as a gain. Since losses loom larger, the minimum selling price exceeds the maximum buying price.
- **Real-World Application**: A mug given to students in an experiment is suddenly valued at ~$7 by owners but only ~$3 by buyers. Real estate: homeowners overvalue their property.
- **Related Concepts**: Loss aversion, status quo bias, reference point.

### 10. The Planning Fallacy
- **Definition**: The systematic tendency to underestimate the time, costs, and risks of future actions while overestimating the benefits. Plans are based on best-case scenarios rather than realistic base rates.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 23. Caused by focusing on the specific plan (inside view) rather than the outcomes of similar projects (outside view). Kahneman's solution: use the outside view (reference class forecasting).
- **Real-World Application**: Major infrastructure projects typically go 50-100% over budget; software projects consistently miss deadlines; individuals underestimate how long it will take to complete a thesis.
- **Related Concepts**: Optimism bias, outside view, reference class forecasting, pre-mortem.

### 11. The Conjunction Fallacy
- **Definition**: The logical error of judging that a conjunction of two events is more probable than one of the events alone.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 15 (Linda problem). "Linda is a bank teller" is judged less probable than "Linda is a bank teller and active in the feminist movement" because the latter is more representative of the description. This violates a fundamental law of probability.
- **Real-World Application**: Adding plausible details to a scenario makes it seem more likely, even though each additional detail reduces the probability.
- **Related Concepts**: Representativeness heuristic, conjunction rule, coherence.

### 12. The Peak-End Rule
- **Definition**: The remembering self judges an experience largely by its most intense point (peak) and its end, rather than by the total pleasure or pain averaged over time.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 36. Explains duration neglect: longer painful experiences are not remembered as worse if the end is improved. This is the foundation of the two-selves distinction.
- **Real-World Application**: A colonoscopy patient whose procedure ended with less pain remembered it as less unpleasant, even though it was objectively longer; a vacation remembered as wonderful despite bad weather on most days.
- **Related Concepts**: Duration neglect, experiencing self vs. remembering self, memory bias.

### 13. The Halo Effect
- **Definition**: The tendency for a positive (or negative) impression in one area to influence judgment in unrelated areas.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 7. Explained by System 1's drive for coherence. An attractive person is judged as more competent; a successful company is assumed to have good management in all areas.
- **Real-World Application**: Hiring decisions influenced by candidate attractiveness or confidence rather than relevant skills; stock analysts rating a company highly because they like its CEO.
- **Related Concepts**: Coherence seeking, affect heuristic, confirmation bias.

### 14. Regression to the Mean
- **Definition**: After an extreme event, the next outcome is likely to be closer to the average. This statistical phenomenon is often misinterpreted as a causal effect.
- **In the Book**: Chapter 17. Kahneman shows that even experts (flight instructors) misunderstand regression: punishing poor performance seems to work (the next performance regresses upward), and rewarding good performance seems to backfire (the next performance regresses downward).
- **Real-World Application**: A student who does exceptionally well on one exam is likely to do closer to their average on the next, regardless of teaching quality.
- **Related Concepts**: Statistical regression, reversion to the mean, illusory correlation.
