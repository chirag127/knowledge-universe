### About the Author

**Sharon M. Kaye** is Associate Professor of Philosophy at John Carroll University
in Ohio. She specializes in medieval philosophy and logic, and is also the author
of *Medieval Philosophy: A Beginner's Guide* (Oneworld, 2008).

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Strengths
- **Exceptionally clear** — explains formal logic concepts (distribution,
  validity, Square of Opposition) in accessible language
- **Concise** — covers both formal and informal logic in under 200 pages
- **Exercises with answer key** — enables self-study
- **Practical real-world examples** — uses newspaper extracts, advertisements,
  everyday arguments
- **Chapter 5 is unique** — most logic books stop at evaluation; Kaye teaches
  constructive argument development
- **Glossary and quick references** — useful for ongoing reference

### Weaknesses / Criticism
- **Very short** — some topics (especially sentential logic) feel rushed
- **No coverage** of inductive logic, statistical reasoning, or probability
- **Limited fallacy coverage** compared to dedicated fallacy books
- **Some exercises feel too easy** — does not always challenge the reader
- **No digital resources** — the 2009 publication predates widespread online
  companion materials
- **Western focus** — examples are primarily from English-language contexts

### Who Should Read This
- **Highly recommended for**: Absolute beginners to logic; high school and
  college students; anyone who wants a quick, clear introduction to critical
  thinking
- **Less suitable for**: Those already familiar with formal logic; those
  wanting comprehensive coverage of fallacies or inductive reasoning
- **Best approach**: Read straight through, do ALL exercises, then supplement
  with a fallacy-focused book (e.g., *Nonsense* by Gula) and a statistics book

### Key Quotes
- "Critical thinking is the art of thinking about thinking."
- "The goal of an argument is not to win, but to reach the truth."
- "Always interpret an opponent's argument in its strongest, most reasonable
  form."
- "The unexamined argument is not worth winning."
