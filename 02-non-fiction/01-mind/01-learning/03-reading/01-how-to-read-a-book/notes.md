### About the Authors

**Mortimer J. Adler** (1902–2001) was an American philosopher, educator, and
author. A proponent of the Great Books of the Western World, he taught at Columbia
University and the University of Chicago, and edited the 54-volume *Great Books of
the Western World* (including the *Syntopicon*). He wrote dozens of books aimed at
the general reader.

**Charles Van Doren** (1926–2019) was an American writer and editor who
co-authored the revised edition. He worked at the Encyclopedia Britannica
Institute and was an editor for the *Great Books of the Western World* series.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Strengths
- The four-level framework is genuinely useful and widely applicable
- The idea of "inspectional reading" alone is worth the price — a systematic way
  to decide what to read deeply
- The 15 rules of analytical reading, while dense, provide a complete toolkit for
  mastering non-fiction
- Genre-specific advice (Chapters 13–19) is practical and insightful
- Syntopical reading is a genuinely original concept that few other books address

### Weaknesses / Criticism
- **Verbosity**: Many readers find the book repetitive and longer than necessary
- **Western-centric**: The reading list and examples are exclusively Western — the
  authors acknowledge this but do not address it
- **Dated writing style**: "Men" as generic pronoun, assumptions about the reader's
  educational background
- **Dense and academic**: Despite Adler's claim of writing for "Joe Doakes," the
  book reads like a philosophy textbook
- **Fiction sections are weak**: The chapters on imaginative literature,
  especially plays and poetry, are considered less useful than those on non-fiction
- **Mixed reviews on practical applicability**: Some readers find the 15 rules
  overwhelming and impractical for everyday reading

### Who Should Read This
- **Highly recommended for**: Students, academics, serious non-fiction readers,
  self-educators, anyone who wants to read more deeply
- **Less suitable for**: Casual readers, those primarily interested in fiction,
  those who want speed-reading techniques only
- **Best read**: After inspectional reading of the whole book, then analytical
  reading of Parts I and II (skip Part III if not needed), and syntopical reading
  of Part IV

### Key Quotes
- "The reader who fails to ponder what he has read is like a person who puts food
  into his mouth without chewing it."
- "You must be able to say, with reasonable certainty, 'I understand,' before you
  can say any one of the following: 'I agree,' or 'I disagree,' or 'I suspend
  judgment.'"
- "True freedom is impossible without a mind made free by discipline."
- "In the case of good books, the point is not to see how many of them you can
  get through, but rather how many can get through to you."
