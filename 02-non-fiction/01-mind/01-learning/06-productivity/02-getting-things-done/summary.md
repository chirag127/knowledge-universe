## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

The human mind is not designed to hold task lists—it is designed to hold
ideas and process them. Stress and overwhelm come from "open loops"
(undecided commitments) cluttering the mind. GTD provides a complete
workflow management system: capture everything into a trusted external
system, clarify what each item means and the next action required,
organize it into appropriate categories, review regularly, and engage
with trust. The result is a "mind like water"—a relaxed, productive
state where you know exactly what to do and when.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### Pillar 1: The Five Stages of Workflow Mastery (Chapter 2)
**Collect → Process → Organize → Review → Do**. The core horizontal
workflow that applies to all incoming "stuff." Each stage has specific
rules and best practices.

### Pillar 2: The Five Phases of Project Planning (Chapter 3)
The vertical planning model: **Purpose → Vision → Brainstorming →
Organizing → Next Actions**. Used for more complex projects requiring
deeper thinking.

### Pillar 3: The Collection Habit (Chapter 5/11)
Anything that has your attention must be captured in a trusted external
system. The mind is for having ideas, not holding them.

### Pillar 4: The Processing Workflow (Chapter 6)
Process each inbox item one at a time. Ask: "What is it?" → "Is it
actionable?" If yes → do it (≤2 min), delegate it, or defer it. If no →
trash, incubate, or reference.

### Pillar 5: The Organizing System (Chapter 7)
Organize into: Next Actions list, Projects list, Waiting For list,
Someday/Maybe list, Calendar, and Reference system. Context-based lists.

### Pillar 6: The Weekly Review (Chapter 8)
The critical success factor. A weekly ritual to process all loose ends,
update all lists, and get current and clear. Without this, the system
breaks down.

### Pillar 7: The Natural Planning Model (Chapter 10)
How successful people naturally plan projects: **Define purpose and
principles → Outcome vision → Brainstorm → Organize → Identify next
actions**.

### Pillar 8: Horizons of Focus
Six horizons from ground-level actions to life purpose: **Current
Actions → Projects → Areas of Focus → 1-2 Year Goals → 3-5 Year Vision
→ Life Purpose**.

## 🔗 Connections to Other Books

### 1. Deep Work by Cal Newport
GTD handles the shallow work management so you can practice deep work.
Newport's shutdown ritual echoes GTD's complete capture principle.

### 2. Atomic Habits by James Clear
Clear's systems-for-change philosophy complements GTD. GTD provides the
trusted external system; Clear provides the habit-building to maintain it.

### 3. The ONE Thing by Gary Keller
Keller's focus on one priority challenges GTD's comprehensive capture.
GTD says "capture everything"; ONE Thing says "prioritize ruthlessly."

### 4. Essentialism by Greg McKeown
Essentialism helps filter what goes into your GTD system. McKeown's
"less but better" pairs with Allen's "capture everything."

### 5. The Checklist Manifesto by Atul Gawande
Both books advocate systematic processes to reduce cognitive load.
Gawande's checklists parallel GTD's lists and workflows.
