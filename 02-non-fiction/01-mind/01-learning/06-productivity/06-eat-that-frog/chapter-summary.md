# Eat That Frog!: 21 Great Ways to Stop Procrastinating and Get More Done in Less Time — Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

## Reading Guidance

**Structure**: Introduction + 21 self-contained chapters + Conclusion.
Each chapter presents one technique. They are designed to be read
sequentially but also work as standalone references. The core metaphor
("eat your frog") is introduced early; each chapter reinforces it from
a different angle.

---

## Introduction

**Core Question**: What is your "frog" and why should you eat it first?

**What it Covers**: The central metaphor: your "frog" is your biggest,
most important task—the one you're most likely to procrastinate on.
Eating it first thing each morning means your worst task is behind you
and everything else becomes easier. Tracy introduces the three Ds:
**Decision** (decide to eat frogs), **Discipline** (force yourself),
**Determination** (follow through).

**Key Concepts**: Frog metaphor; The three Ds.

**Takeaway**: If you have to eat two frogs, eat the ugliest one first.

## The 21 Techniques

### Chapter 1 — Set the Table

**Core Question**: Why is clarity essential for productivity?

**What it Covers**: Decide exactly what you want. Write down your goals,
make them specific and measurable, and set deadlines. Clarity is 80% of
success. Thinking on paper forces clarity. Apply to every goal in your
life: career, family, financial, health.

**Key Concepts**: Written goals; Clarity; Deadlines; Goal-setting
formula (Write it down, set deadline, make plan, do daily action).

**Takeaway**: A goal without a plan is just a wish.

### Chapter 2 — Plan Every Day in Advance

**Core Question**: How do you structure your day for maximum productivity?

**What it Covers**: The 10/90 Rule: 10% of time spent planning saves
90% of execution time. Plan every day the night before. Write a list,
prioritize it, and work on the highest-value item first.

**Key Concepts**: 10/90 Rule; Nightly planning; Prioritized to-do list.

**Takeaway**: Every minute spent planning saves 10 minutes in execution.

### Chapter 3 — Apply the 80/20 Rule to Everything

**Core Question**: Which tasks produce the most results?

**What it Covers**: 20% of your activities produce 80% of your results.
Resist the temptation to "clear the small stuff" first. Identify your
top 20% and work on them before anything else.

**Key Concepts**: Pareto Principle; Focus on the vital 20%; Don't clear
the small stuff first.

**Takeaway**: Focus your energy on the 20% that matters.

### Chapter 4 — Consider the Consequences

**Core Question**: How do you determine true priority?

**What it Covers**: The most important task is the one with the most
significant consequences if done—or not done. Future-oriented thinking:
what you do today determines your tomorrow. Ask: "What are the
potential consequences of doing or not doing this task?"

**Key Concepts**: Consequences-based prioritization; Long-term thinking.

**Takeaway**: Tasks with the biggest consequences are your frogs.

### Chapter 5 — Practice Creative Procrastination

**Core Question**: Is it okay to put off some tasks?

**What it Covers**: You can't do everything. Creative Procrastination is
the art of deliberately deciding what NOT to do now. You must actively
choose to abandon low-value tasks, not just postpone them.

**Key Concepts**: Creative procrastination; Strategic abandonment;
Saying no to low-value activities.

**Takeaway**: Put off what doesn't matter so you can do what does.

### Chapter 6 — Use the ABCDE Method Continually

**Core Question**: How do you rank your tasks by priority?

**What it Covers**: Rank tasks from A to E: A = Must do (serious
consequences), B = Should do (mild consequences), C = Nice to do (no
consequences), D = Delegate, E = Eliminate. Never do a B when an A is
unfinished. Force rank A tasks as A-1, A-2, etc.

**Key Concepts**: ABCDE Method; Priority ranking; "Never do B when A is
unfinished."

**Takeaway**: Your most important task is your A-1. Do it first.

### Chapter 7 — Focus on Key Result Areas

**Core Question**: What specific results must you produce?

**What it Covers**: Identify the 5-7 key result areas (KRAs) of your
work. Rate yourself on each. Identify your weakest KRA—it's the
limiting factor. Improve it and your overall performance improves.
Outsourcing or delegating weak areas is a viable strategy.

**Key Concepts**: Key Result Areas; Self-assessment; Weakest link;
Outsource weakness.

**Takeaway**: Your weakest KRA determines your overall effectiveness.

### Chapter 8 — The Law of Three

**Core Question**: What are the three most important things you do?

**What it Covers**: Identify the three most valuable contributions you
make to your organization. Concentrate your time and energy on these
three areas. Use them to decide what to work on each day.

**Key Concepts**: Law of Three; Top three contributions; Concentrate
on highest value.

**Takeaway**: Most of your value comes from 2-3 key activities.

### Chapter 9 — Prepare Thoroughly Before You Begin

**Core Question**: How do you set yourself up for success?

**What it Covers**: Have everything you need ready before you start.
Create a workspace that supports productivity. Clear your desk—clutter
is a mental distraction. Prepare all materials, tools, and information
before beginning.

**Key Concepts**: Preparation; Workspace setup; Friction removal.

**Takeaway**: A well-prepared workspace makes the frog easier to eat.

### Chapter 10 — Take It One Oil Barrel at a Time

**Core Question**: How do you eat a very large frog?

**What it Covers**: Break large, complex tasks into smaller manageable
steps. Tackle them one piece at a time. The journey of a thousand miles
begins with a single step. Focus on "the next thing."

**Key Concepts**: Step-by-step; Decomposition; Small incremental
progress.

**Examples**: Atlas Shrugged written one page at a time.

**Takeaway**: You can't eat the whole frog in one bite—take it one
piece at a time.

### Chapter 11 — Upgrade Your Key Skills

**Core Question**: How does skill improvement reduce procrastination?

**What it Covers**: The more skilled you are at a task, the less you
procrastinate. Continuous learning in key areas reduces fear and
increases confidence. Commit to lifelong learning. The "learning-curve"
effect: doing tasks we're good at is pleasurable.

**Key Concepts**: Skill development reduces procrastination; Continuous
improvement; Lifelong learning.

**Takeaway**: The more competent you become, the easier it is to eat
your frog.

### Chapter 12 — Leverage Your Special Talents

**Core Question**: Should you focus on your strengths or weaknesses?

**What it Covers**: Identify what you do exceptionally well. Spend the
majority of your time doing what you're uniquely talented at. Delegate
or outsource the rest. Work in your zone of genius.

**Key Concepts**: Unique talents; Zone of genius; Delegate everything
else.

**Takeaway**: Do what you're best at; let others do the rest.

### Chapter 13 — Identify Your Key Constraints

**Core Question**: What is holding you back?

**What it Covers**: Identify the single biggest constraint or bottleneck
limiting your speed. Ask: "What is holding me back?" Focus on removing
it. Internal constraints (your skills/habits) are usually more
controllable than external ones.

**Key Concepts**: Key constraint; Bottleneck analysis; 80/20 applied
to constraints; Internal vs. external constraints.

**Takeaway**: Find the constraint and remove it to get unstuck.

### Chapter 14 — Put the Pressure on Yourself

**Core Question**: How do you create internal motivation and deadlines?

**What it Covers**: Self-imposed deadlines are more powerful than
external ones. Create your own pressure by setting tight deadlines and
rewarding yourself for meeting them. Work as if you have only one day
to get your most important tasks done.

**Key Concepts**: Self-imposed deadlines; Internal pressure; Work in
time-limited sprints.

**Takeaway**: Set your own deadlines and hold yourself accountable.

### Chapter 15 — Maximize Your Personal Power

**Core Question**: How do you sustain high energy?

**What it Covers**: Physical energy is the foundation of productivity.
Get sufficient sleep (7-8 hours), eat healthy meals (low sugar, high
protein), exercise regularly, and take breaks. Structure your day
around your energy cycles.

**Key Concepts**: Sleep, diet, exercise; Energy management; Work during
peak energy hours; Strategic breaks.

**Takeaway**: Productivity is energy management, not just time management.

### Chapter 16 — Motivate Yourself into Action

**Core Question**: How do you get yourself to start?

**What it Covers**: Use positive self-talk, affirmations, and visual-
ization. Focus on the rewards of completing the task rather than the
discomfort of doing it. Your attitude determines your motivation.
Tell yourself "I can do it!"

**Key Concepts**: Positive self-talk; Visualization; Focus on rewards;
"I can do it" mindset.

**Takeaway**: Your inner dialogue shapes your motivation.

### Chapter 17 — Get Out of the Technological Time Sinks

**Core Question**: How do you take control of technology instead of
letting it control you?

**What it Covers**: Technology (email, social media, smartphones) can be
a massive time sink. Use intentionality: check email on a schedule, not
constantly. Disconnect regularly. Use the "zero-based" approach: would
you start this task/process if you weren't already doing it?

**Key Concepts**: Technological discipline; Scheduled checking; Zero-
based thinking; Digital disconnection.

**Takeaway**: Control your technology or it will control you.

### Chapter 18 — Slice and Dice the Task

**Core Question**: How do you make daunting tasks approachable?

**What it Covers**: Slice and dice large tasks into smaller sub-tasks
and focus on completing just one slice. The "salami slice" method:
every time you complete a slice, you build momentum and confidence.
Focus on the first slice.

**Key Concepts**: Salami slice method; Task decomposition; Build
momentum.

**Takeaway**: Don't think about the whole task—think about the next
slice.

### Chapter 19 — Create Large Chunks of Time

**Core Question**: How do you get focused work done?

**What it Covers**: Schedule large, uninterrupted blocks of time for
your most important tasks. Use time-blocking. Work in 60-90 minute
sessions with short breaks. Protect this time ruthlessly.

**Key Concepts**: Time blocking; Large uninterrupted work sessions;
Protected focus time.

**Takeaway**: Major tasks require major time blocks. Schedule them.

### Chapter 20 — Develop a Sense of Urgency

**Core Question**: How do you speed up your pace of work?

**What it Covers**: Cultivate a fast tempo. Develop a bias for action.
The faster you work, the more energy and momentum you build. A sense of
urgency gets more done and feels better than sluggishness.

**Key Concepts**: Bias for action; Fast tempo; Momentum begets
momentum.

**Takeaway**: Move fast. Urgency is a choice.

### Chapter 21 — Single Handle Every Task

**Core Question**: Should you start and finish tasks in one session?

**What it Covers**: Once you start a major task, discipline yourself to
finish it before moving on. "Single-handling" creates momentum and
satisfaction. Interruptions are the enemy of completion. Complete the
cycle.

**Key Concepts**: Single handling; Completion; Uninterrupted task
execution.

**Takeaway**: Start it, finish it, don't go back.

---

### Conclusion: Putting It All Together

**Core Question**: How do you integrate all 21 principles into your
life?

**What it Covers**: Review the key points. The decision to eat frogs
and the discipline to follow through will change your life. The 21
principles are a toolkit—start with one and build from there.

**Key Concepts**: Integration; Starting with one technique; Consistency.

**Takeaway**: The key is not knowing—it's doing. Start today.
