## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Skepticism is not cynicism — it is a systematic method for distinguishing reliable knowledge from error, fraud, and wishful thinking. Novella updates and expands the skeptical tradition (from Sagan to Randi) for the 21st century, where misinformation spreads faster than ever. The core toolkit has three components: understanding cognitive biases that distort our thinking (confirmation bias, availability heuristic, etc.), recognizing logical fallacies that undermine arguments (ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, etc.), and applying the scientific method as the gold standard for evaluating claims. Each of these is taught not as abstract theory but as practical, quotable mental habits — like "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence" and "the burden of proof is on the claimant." The book also addresses specific domains where skepticism is most needed: alternative medicine, conspiracy theories, the paranormal, and the misinformation ecosystem of the internet.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition | In the Book | Real-World Application | Related Concepts |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Scientific Skepticism** | The application of reason and critical thinking to any claim | The book's overarching framework — not denial but evidence-based evaluation | Health claims, political rhetoric, news stories | Critical thinking |
| **Baloney Detection** | Systematic evaluation of claims using cognitive bias awareness, logical fallacies, and scientific method | The core skill the book aims to teach | Everyday evaluation of news, ads, political claims | Sagan's Baloney Detection Kit |
| **Denialism** | The rejection of established scientific consensus for ideological or commercial reasons | Ch. 16-18: evolution denial, climate denial, anti-vaccine | Recognizing when skepticism has crossed into motivated denial | Manufactured controversy |
| **Argument from Authority** | Fallacy of believing a claim because an authority says it | Ch. 5: authorities are often wrong; science relies on evidence, not authority | "Doctors recommend..." advertising | Appeal to authority |
