## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Being wrong is not a rare accident but the default state of human cognition, yet we treat it as an abnormality because wrongness has no distinct phenomenology — it feels exactly like being right until the moment of discovery. Schulz takes us through the science of error (how our senses, memory, and beliefs systematically mislead us) and the human experience of error (what happens when we discover we are wrong and how we react). She argues that our cultural obsession with being right and our terror of being wrong are the true sources of much human misery — from failed relationships to political gridlock to wrongful convictions. The alternative is "wrongology": learning to live comfortably with fallibility, treating error not as failure but as the gateway to growth, creativity, and genuine understanding.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition | In the Book | Real-World Application | Related Concepts |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Wrongology** | The systematic study of error — what it means, how it feels, what it reveals | The book's central project; a call to take error seriously as a subject of inquiry | Understanding the psychology of mistakes in your organization | Error studies |
| **Phenomenology of Error** | The subjective experience of being wrong — or rather, the absence of distinctive experience | Being wrong feels exactly like being right; only the discovery of error reveals it | Why we cannot "feel" ourselves making errors | Meta-cognition |
| **Error as Default** | The thesis that error is the normal state of human cognition | We are wrong most of the time about most things; being right is the exception | Intellectual humility: assume you are probably wrong | Fallibilism |
| **Error Blindness** | The inability to detect one's own errors in real time | There is no "error signal" in consciousness; we only discover errors after the fact | Why it's so hard to catch our own mistakes | Blind spot bias |
| **Moral Error** | The experience of realizing one's moral beliefs or actions were wrong | The emotional impact of moral error is different from factual error — involves guilt and shame | Realizing you were wrong about a person; changing political views | Moral foundations |
| **Anatomy of Error** | The typical progression of how errors form, persist, and are discovered | Four stages: assumption → belief → disconfirmation → discovery or denial | Understanding the structure of mistakes in complex systems | Error management |
| **Intellectual Humility** | The capacity to recognize the limits of one's knowledge | The central virtue of wrongology; the alternative to the arrogance of certainty | Admitting "I don't know" and "I was wrong" | Epistemic humility |
