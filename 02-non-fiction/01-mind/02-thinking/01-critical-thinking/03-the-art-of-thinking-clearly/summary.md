## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

The human brain did not evolve for rationality — it evolved for survival in ancestral environments, which means it takes cognitive shortcuts (heuristics) that systematically produce errors in modern contexts. Dobelli catalogs 99 of these errors — from survivorship bias (we see winners, not losers) to the sunk cost fallacy (we persist with failing investments because we've already committed resources) — showing how they distort perception, judgment, and decision-making in business, relationships, and everyday life. The book's thesis is that awareness is partial protection: knowing that you are susceptible to confirmation bias does not make you immune, but it creates the possibility of pausing, reflecting, and sometimes overriding the automatic response. The format of short, self-contained chapters (2-3 pages each) mirrors the modular nature of the biases themselves — each is a distinct mental glitch with its own trigger and remedy.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition | In the Book | Real-World Application | Related Concepts |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Survivorship Bias** | Overestimating success probability by focusing on survivors while ignoring failures | Ch. 1: "Why you should visit cemeteries" — unsuccessful people are invisible | Startup success stories ignore the 90%+ that fail; stock tips from millionaires | Selection bias |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Continuing an endeavor because of past investment, even when future costs exceed benefits | Ch. 37: "Beware of the investment that is already lost" | Staying in bad relationships, continuing failing projects | Loss aversion, Escalation of commitment |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Tendency to interpret new evidence as confirming existing beliefs | Ch. 27: "The world is simple — but that's because you ignore contradictory evidence" | Echo chambers, selective reading, political polarization | Myside bias |
| **Availability Heuristic** | Judging likelihood by ease of recall — vivid events seem more common | Ch. 3: "Why we overestimate plane crashes" | Fear of terrorism vs. car accidents; media-driven risk perception | Salience bias |
| **Hindsight Bias** | Seeing past events as having been predictable | Ch. 22: "Why we think we knew it all along" | Post-game analysis, historical second-guessing, "I told you so" | Creeping determinism |
| **Anchoring Effect** | The first number encountered influences subsequent judgments | Ch. 19: "Why the first number sticks" | Salary negotiations, pricing, estimating | Priming |
| **Herd Behavior** | Following the crowd without independent thought | Ch. 28: "Why we follow the herd" | Stock market bubbles, fashion, consumer trends | Social proof |
| **Overconfidence Effect** | Systematic overestimation of one's knowledge, abilities, or prediction accuracy | Ch. 4: "Why we overestimate our knowledge" | 93% of Americans think they are above-average drivers | Dunning-Kruger effect |
| **Framing Effect** | The way information is presented changes how it is evaluated | Ch. 13: "How to turn a disadvantage into an advantage" | "90% fat-free" vs. "10% fat"; pricing strategies | Loss/gain framing |
| **Narrative Fallacy** | The tendency to weave disparate facts into a coherent story | Ch. 23: "Why stories seduce us" | Stock market "explanations," business success stories, history | Pattern seeking |
