# The Half-Life of Facts: Why Everything We Know Has an Expiration Date
— Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

## Reading Guidance

The book is structured as a narrative exploration of the concept that knowledge changes. Each chapter examines a different aspect of how and why facts evolve. Read linearly.

---

## Chapter-by-Chapter Breakdown

### Chapter 1: The Half-Life of Facts

- **Core Question**: Do facts have predictable lifetimes?
- **What it Covers**: The central concept—knowledge decays over time like radioactive isotopes; measuring the half-life of facts in different fields.
- **Key Concepts**: Half-life, knowledge decay, scientometrics.
- **Examples**: Medical facts have a half-life of ~45 years; the changing understanding of ulcers (stress/bacteria); Pluto's demotion from planet status.
- **Takeaway**: Facts have measurable lifetimes; we should expect and plan for knowledge change.
- **Thesis Connection**: Introduces the book's central metaphor and framework.

### Chapter 2: The Pace of Discovery

- **Core Question**: How fast is knowledge growing?
- **What it Covers**: Exponential growth of scientific knowledge; how to measure the pace of discovery.
- **Key Concepts**: Exponential growth, doubling time, knowledge explosion.
- **Examples**: The number of scientific papers doubling every 15 years; the growth of GenBank (DNA sequences); Moore's Law.
- **Takeaway**: Knowledge is growing exponentially across almost all fields.
- **Thesis Connection**: Explains why facts change faster than ever.

### Chapter 3: The Asymptote of Truth

- **Core Question**: Do measurements of the world converge or diverge?
- **What it Covers**: How scientific measurements of fundamental constants have become more precise over time.
- **Key Concepts**: Asymptote, measurement convergence, uncertainty reduction.
- **Examples**: The speed of light (increasingly precise measurements converging on 299,792,458 m/s); the Hubble constant; measuring the distance to the moon.
- **Takeaway**: For some facts, we get closer and closer to truth—but never perfectly there.
- **Thesis Connection**: Some knowledge asymptotically approaches truth while other knowledge is overturned.

### Chapter 4: Moore's Law of Everything

- **Core Question**: What domains follow exponential growth patterns?
- **What it Covers**: The prevalence of exponential growth across diverse knowledge domains.
- **Key Concepts**: Exponential growth, superexponential growth, growth limits.
- **Examples**: GenBank (DNA sequences doubling every 18 months); the number of known chemical compounds; the growth of the internet; Moore's Law in computing.
- **Takeaway**: Many seemingly unrelated domains follow similar exponential growth trajectories.
- **Thesis Connection**: The rate of knowledge change follows predictable mathematical patterns.

### Chapter 5: The Spread of Facts

- **Core Question**: How does new knowledge diffuse through society?
- **What it Covers**: The sociology of knowledge diffusion; how scientific discoveries spread.
- **Key Concepts**: Diffusion, adoption curves, information cascades, opinion leaders.
- **Examples**: The adoption of medical innovations; how the discovery of the structure of DNA spread; the diffusion of "eating local" movements.
- **Takeaway**: Facts don't spread instantly—they follow predictable S-curves of adoption.
- **Thesis Connection**: The dynamics of knowledge spread are as important as the facts themselves.

### Chapter 6: Hidden Knowledge

- **Core Question**: What do we know that we don't know we know?
- **What it Covers**: Undiscovered public knowledge—facts that exist in the scientific literature but haven't been connected.
- **Key Concepts**: Hidden knowledge, undiscovered public knowledge, data mining.
- **Examples**: Swanson's discovery of a link between fish oil and Raynaud's disease through literature mining; the potential of data integration.
- **Takeaway**: Much knowledge already exists but is hidden in disconnected datasets and publications.
- **Thesis Connection**: The challenge isn't always discovering new facts but connecting existing ones.

### Chapter 7: Fact Phase Transitions

- **Core Question**: How do facts change state from hypothesis to accepted truth?
- **What it Covers**: The process by which ideas become accepted as facts; the sociology of scientific consensus.
- **Key Concepts**: Phase transition, consensus formation, paradigm shift.
- **Examples**: The acceptance of continental drift (Wegener); the demotion of Pluto; the discovery of the Higgs boson.
- **Takeaway**: Facts undergo phase transitions—they are not born as facts but become facts through social and scientific processes.
- **Thesis Connection**: The lifecycle of a fact has distinct stages.

### Chapter 8: Mount Everest and the Discovery of Error

- **Core Question**: How do we discover and correct errors in knowledge?
- **What it Covers**: The process of error detection and correction; systematic biases in knowledge.
- **Key Concepts**: Error discovery, retractions, systematic bias, correction mechanisms.
- **Examples**: The discovery that Mount Everest's height changed; the retraction of the MMR vaccine/autism study; the replication crisis in psychology.
- **Takeaway**: Error detection is a vital part of the knowledge ecosystem—but it happens slowly.
- **Thesis Connection**: The half-life of false facts may be longer than we'd like.

### Chapter 9: The Human Side of Facts

- **Core Question**: How do human biases and institutions affect knowledge change?
- **What it Covers**: The role of scientists, institutions, and social factors in shaping what we know.
- **Key Concepts**: Institutional inertia, prestige bias, conflict of interest, groupthink.
- **Examples**: The resistance to Semmelweis's hand-washing discovery; the influence of funding sources on research outcomes; the role of prestige in scientific publishing.
- **Takeaway**: Facts exist within human systems that can accelerate or resist knowledge change.
- **Thesis Connection**: Knowledge change is not purely rational—it's shaped by human factors.

### Chapter 10: At the Edge of What We Know

- **Core Question**: What are the frontiers of knowledge change?
- **What it Covers**: Looking forward; how the study of knowledge change can help us manage our ignorance.
- **Key Concepts**: Managing ignorance, prediction markets, crowdsourcing science.
- **Examples**: The use of prediction markets in science; citizen science projects (Galaxy Zoo); the Polymath project.
- **Takeaway**: We can predict some aspects of knowledge change and use that to make better decisions.
- **Thesis Connection**: The practical implications of understanding fact half-lives.
