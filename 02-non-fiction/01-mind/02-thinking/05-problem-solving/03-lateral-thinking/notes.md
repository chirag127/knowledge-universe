### Author

**Edward de Bono** (1933–2020) — Maltese physician, psychologist, author, and inventor. Coined the term "lateral thinking." Wrote 62+ books translated into 37+ languages. Developed the Six Thinking Hats, Direct Attention Thinking Tools (CoRT), and other thinking frameworks. Advisor to governments and corporations worldwide. Degrees: MD (University of Malta), PhD (University of Oxford), DPhil (University of Cambridge).

### Editions Note

The original 1970 edition was titled *Lateral Thinking: Creativity Step by Step*. Later editions were retitled *Lateral Thinking: A Textbook of Creativity* (Penguin, 1977 onward). Chapter structure is identical.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Techniques to Practice

1. **Quota Setting** — Before evaluating any solution, force yourself to generate N alternatives (N = 10, 20, 30+)
2. **Suspended Judgment** — When an idea comes, do not evaluate it. Write it down. Let it sit. Explore it.
3. **Dominant Idea Hunt** — For any problem, ask: "What is the single assumption I'm making that most constrains my thinking?"
4. **Reversal Practice** — Take any conventional relationship and reverse it. What new questions emerge?
5. **Random Word Provocation** — Open a dictionary to a random page. Connect that word to your problem.
6. **"Po" Experiments** — Make deliberately absurd statements about your problem prefixed with "Po." See where they lead.

### Key Takeaways for Practice

- Lateral thinking is a skill, not a personality trait — practice it deliberately
- Don't confuse generating ideas with judging them — separate the phases
- The best way to get a new idea is to deliberately disrupt your current patterns
- Random input is a feature, not a bug — welcome irrelevance
- Categories and labels are conveniences, not truths — treat them as fluid
- Education overemphasizes vertical thinking; balance is essential

### Gaps & Critiques

- Some techniques feel dated in framing (1970s examples)
- Less structure than modern creativity methodologies (e.g., design thinking)
- The theoretical basis (neural pattern formation) is speculative by modern cognitive science standards
- Limited discussion of implementation and organizational application
- Fewer case studies than contemporary readers might expect
