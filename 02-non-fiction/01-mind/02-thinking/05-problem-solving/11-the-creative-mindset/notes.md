## 📝 Personal Notes

### The CREATE Framework in Action

| Skill | How to Practice |
|-------|----------------|
| **C**oncentrate | Write a problem statement in 3 different ways before starting |
| **R**eplicate | Find 3 examples of similar problems solved in other industries |
| **E**laborate | Take an initial idea and add 10 features or variations |
| **A**ssociate | Ask "What is this problem like?" — find an analogy from nature, sports, cooking, etc. |
| **T**ranslate | Craft a 60-second story about your idea that anyone can understand |
| **E**valuate | Rank ideas against 3 explicit criteria before making a decision |

### Key Takeaways for Practice

- The most important creative skill is Concentrate — get the problem right
- Originality is overrated; replication and adaptation produce most innovation
- Elaboration turns good ideas into great ones — don't stop at the first version
- Analogies are the most powerful creative technique — train yourself to see patterns across domains
- An idea has no value until it is effectively communicated — invest in storytelling
- Evaluation is part of the creative process, not its opposite
- Practice all six skills regularly — like any skill set, they atrophy without use

### Gaps & Critiques

- Relatively short (168 pages) — each skill gets limited depth
- The CREATE acronym, while memorable, can feel like a marketing device
- More examples from large enterprises than small businesses or individual practitioners
- Limited discussion of the research basis for each skill
- Does not address organizational or systemic barriers to creativity
- Some overlap with existing creativity frameworks (SCAMPER, design thinking, etc.)
