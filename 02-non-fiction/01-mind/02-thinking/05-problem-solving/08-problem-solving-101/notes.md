### Author

**Ken Watanabe** — Japanese author and former McKinsey & Company consultant. MBA from Harvard Business School. Originally wrote the book to help shift Japanese education from memorization to critical thinking. Surprised by its massive success with adult readers.

### Origin Story

The book was originally created as a teaching guide for Japanese schoolchildren. Watanabe adapted McKinsey problem-solving techniques into a kid-friendly format. When published in Japan in 2007, it became an unexpected bestseller, topping business book charts. The English edition followed in 2009.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Characters (Case Study Scenarios)

- **Class 1**: Generic child scenario (allowance/money management)
- **Class 2**: A rock band trying to increase concert attendance
- **Class 3**: An aspiring animator saving for a computer
- **Class 4**: A student choosing between soccer schools

Note: The English edition replaced the original Japanese characters (Mushroom, Eggplant, Tofu, etc.) with more generic scenarios for international audiences.

### Key Takeaways for Practice

- The four-step process is universal: identify → analyze → solve → implement
- Logic trees make any problem manageable — draw them before jumping to solutions
- Root cause analysis: ask "why" 3-5 times to go from symptom to cause
- Hypothesis-driven thinking prevents analysis paralysis
- A structured decision matrix makes tough choices objective
- The tools are simple enough to teach a child and sophisticated enough for business

### Gaps & Critiques

- Very basic — experienced problem solvers will find little new
- The child-oriented framing can feel condescending to some adults
- Case studies are simplified and may not reflect real-world complexity
- Does not address team problem solving, cognitive biases, or organizational dynamics
- No coverage of advanced analytical techniques (statistics, modeling, etc.)
- Very short (128 pages) — more of a pamphlet than a comprehensive book
