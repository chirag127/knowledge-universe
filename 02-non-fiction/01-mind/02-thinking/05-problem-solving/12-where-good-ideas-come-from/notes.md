### Author

**Steven Johnson** (1968–) — American science writer and media theorist. Author of 13 books including *The Ghost Map* (2006), *How We Got to Now* (2014), and *Extra Life* (2021). Host and co-creator of the Emmy-winning PBS/BBC series *How We Got to Now*. Host of the *TED Interview* podcast. Known for his interdisciplinary approach to understanding innovation, urbanism, and cultural evolution. Former columnist for *Time*, *Wired*, and *The New York Times*.

### Related Books

Johnson considers *Where Good Ideas Come From* part of an informal trilogy:
1. *The Ghost Map* (2006) — tight-focus case study of cholera and urban innovation
2. *The Invention of Air* (2008) — tight-focus case study of Joseph Priestley
3. *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010) — the wider theoretical framework

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Most Memorable Stories

1. **Gutenberg's printing press** — the key insight (the wine press) came from a domain completely unrelated to bookmaking
2. **Darwin's notebooks** — natural selection theory developed over 20+ years of slow hunch cultivation
3. **Joseph Priestley and oxygen** — the power of liquid networks (the Lunar Society)
4. **GPS invention** — accidental exaptation of microwave technology by two twenty-somethings
5. **Coral reefs** — the biological parallel: the most diverse ecosystems produce the most innovation

### Key Takeaways for Practice

- Put yourself at the edge of the adjacent possible — explore new fields where combinations are emerging
- Create "liquid networks" in your life: diverse conversations, shared spaces, cross-disciplinary connections
- Keep a practice of slow hunches — maintain a notebook, revisit old ideas, let them develop
- Design for serendipity — expose yourself to diverse information sources
- Allow room for error — perfectionism kills the experimentation that produces breakthroughs
- Look for exaptation opportunities — what existing idea can be repurposed for your problem?
- Build on platforms — use existing tools and technologies as foundations for your own innovations

### Gaps & Critiques

- More descriptive than prescriptive — fewer actionable techniques than some readers want
- The "Fourth Quadrant" argument can feel like an appendix rather than an integrated conclusion
- Less attention to organizational barriers to innovation
- Some examples feel cherry-picked to support the thesis
- Does not deeply address the role of market incentives
- The environmental determinism may underplay individual agency and effort
