### Authors

**David Kelley** (1951–) — Founder and chair of IDEO (founded 1978). Founder of the Stanford d.school (Hasso Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford). Donald W. Whittier Professor at Stanford. Pioneer of design thinking and human-centered design.

**Tom Kelley** — Partner at IDEO. Author of *The Art of Innovation* (2001) and *The Ten Faces of Innovation* (2005). Executive Fellow at Haas School of Business, UC Berkeley.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Most Impactful Stories

1. **The "not creative" executive** who discovered his creativity after a simple sketching exercise (Chapter 1)
2. **The kindergarten vs. sixth-grade creativity test** — how education trains creativity out of us
3. **IDEO's shopping cart project** — empathy in action (Chapter 3)
4. **The premature baby incubator prototype** made from car parts in Nepal (Chapter 4)
5. **Kickstarter stories** — ordinary people using creative confidence to launch extraordinary projects

### Key Takeaways for Practice

- The biggest barrier to creativity is the belief that you're not creative
- Creative confidence is built through small wins, not big breakthroughs
- Empathy is the starting point for any creative problem
- Prototype early and often — a bias toward action beats analysis paralysis
- Follow your passion — it's the fuel for sustained creative work
- Build teams with psychological safety for maximum creative output

### Gaps & Critiques

- Heavy reliance on IDEO/d.school anecdotes — may feel like an extended case study
- Less practical step-by-step guidance than some readers expect
- Does not deeply address organizational or structural barriers to creativity
- Some concepts overlap significantly with the Kelleys' earlier books
- The "you can be creative too" message, while true, can feel repetitive
- Limited discussion of cultural and systemic barriers beyond individual psychology
