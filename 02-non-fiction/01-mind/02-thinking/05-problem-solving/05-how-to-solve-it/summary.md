## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Problem solving can be taught and learned through a systematic, four-phase process: (1) understanding the problem, (2) devising a plan, (3) carrying out the plan, and (4) looking back. Polya demonstrates how these phases, guided by specific questions and heuristic strategies, apply to any problem that can be reasoned through. The book is half practical guide (the dialogue) and half reference (the heuristic dictionary), providing thinkers with a toolkit of mental operations — analogy, decomposition, specialization, generalization, working backwards, and more — that make even the most daunting problems manageable.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

**Pillar 1: The Four Phases of Problem Solving**
1. **Understanding the problem**: What is the unknown? What are the data? What is the condition?
2. **Devising a plan**: Find the connection between data and unknown. Consider auxiliary problems.
3. **Carrying out the plan**: Execute the solution with careful, step-by-step checking.
4. **Looking back**: Examine the solution. Can you check the result? Can you derive it differently?

**Pillar 2: The Heuristic Method**
Heuristics are general strategies and mental operations that guide problem solving — not guaranteed formulas but highly probable routes to success. The heuristic dictionary contains dozens of entries (Analogy, Auxiliary elements, Decomposing and recombining, etc.) with practical guidance.

**Pillar 3: The Teacher's Role**
Polya emphasizes the Socratic method: the teacher should guide the student through questions and suggestions, never simply giving answers. The goal is to develop the student's problem-solving faculties.

**Pillar 4: Problem Types & Strategies**
Problems can be "problems to find" (determine the unknown) or "problems to prove" (show a statement is true or false). Different strategies fit different types. The "How to Solve It" list provides a framework of questions that work across types.
