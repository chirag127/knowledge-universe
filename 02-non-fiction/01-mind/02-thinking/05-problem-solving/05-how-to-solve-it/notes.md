### Author

**George Polya** (1887–1985) — Hungarian-American mathematician. Professor at Stanford University (1942–1985). Major contributions to number theory, complex analysis, combinatorics, probability, and mathematical physics. Author of *Mathematics and Plausible Reasoning* and *Mathematical Discovery*. Known for his deep interest in teaching and pedagogy.

### Significance

"How to Solve It" is one of the most cited and influential books on problem solving ever published. It has been continuously in print since 1945 and has sold millions of copies across multiple languages.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### The Four Phases — Executable Summary

**Phase 1: Understand the Problem**
- What is the unknown?
- What are the data?
- What is the condition?
- Is it possible to satisfy the condition?
- Draw a figure. Introduce suitable notation.
- Separate the various parts of the condition.

**Phase 2: Devise a Plan**
- Have you seen it before?
- Do you know a related problem?
- Could you use a related problem?
- Can you restate the problem?
- Go back to definitions.
- Could you solve a part of the problem?
- Did you use all the data?
- Consider auxiliary problems.

**Phase 3: Carry Out the Plan**
- Check each step. Can you see clearly that it is correct?
- Can you prove that it is correct?

**Phase 4: Look Back**
- Can you check the result?
- Can you check the argument?
- Can you derive the result differently?
- Can you use the result (or method) for some other problem?

### Key Takeaways for Practice

- The most important question: "What is the unknown?"
- If you can't solve a problem, find a simpler related problem and solve that first
- The Inventor's Paradox: sometimes a more ambitious plan has a better chance of success
- Working backwards from the goal is often more productive than working forwards from the data
- Always look back — verification and generalization are integral to the solution

### Gaps & Critiques

- Examples are exclusively mathematical — non-mathematicians must translate to their domain
- Some dictionary entries are very brief
- The Socratic dialogue format can feel dated
- Does not address group problem solving, cognitive biases, or organizational context
- The heuristic strategies are domain-general but some require mathematical maturity to fully appreciate
