## Author Background

Dietrich Dorner (b. 1938) is a German psychologist and
professor at the University of Bamberg, where he holds the
chair of Theoretical Psychology. He is a leading researcher
in cognitive psychology, decision theory, and complex
problem-solving. He developed the "Lohhausen" and "Tanaland"
computer simulations that form the empirical backbone of
this book. His work bridges cognitive psychology, systems
theory, and artificial intelligence.

## Personal Notes

- The computer simulations used in the research ran on
1980s-era hardware, yet the findings remain disturbingly
relevant.
- The book is a neglected classic — widely cited but less
widely read than it deserves.
- The English title ("Why Things Go Wrong") is a subtitle
that does not appear in the original German title.
- Dorner's concept of "repair service behavior" anticipates
Senge's "Shifting the Burden" archetype.
- The "exponential blindness" finding has direct relevance
to understanding responses to climate change and pandemics.
- Key critique: the experimental simulations, while
innovative, may not fully replicate the social and
institutional context of real-world decision-making.
