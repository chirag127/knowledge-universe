## The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Why do intelligent, well-meaning people make decisions that
lead to catastrophe? Dorner conducted controlled experiments
using computer simulations (e.g., managing a small African
town, running a dairy factory) and found that failure follows
predictable patterns. When faced with complex systems,
decision-makers tend to: act without sufficient analysis,
focus on one problem at a time (linear thinking), ignore
side effects and long-term consequences, repair symptoms
rather than causes, and become overconfident after initial
success. The "logic of failure" is not irrationality — it is
the systematic application of linear thinking to nonlinear
systems.

## Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. The Failure of Linear Thinking in Nonlinear Systems
The core thesis: humans evolved to handle linear, local,
temporal cause-effect chains, but complex systems operate
nonlinearly with feedback, delays, and interconnectedness.
- **Chapters**: 1-2

### 2. The Experimental Simulations
Dorner's methodology: computer-simulated worlds (Tanaland,
Moro's dairy, Lohhausen). Subjects make decisions over
simulated time; their failures are analyzed.
- **Chapters**: 3-5

### 3. Characteristic Error Patterns
Specific cognitive failures observed repeatedly: inadequate
information gathering, failure to anticipate side effects,
tendency to regulate by "repairing" symptoms, lack of self-
reflection, overstepping, and "ballistic" (single-shot)
decision-making.
- **Chapters**: 4-7

### 4. The Role of Goals and Motivation
How goal structures shape decision quality. Vague goals,
conflicting goals, and goal displacement. The danger of
"satisficing."
- **Chapters**: 8-9

### 5. Decision Styles and Personal Characteristics
Not all people fail equally. Characteristics of successful
vs. unsuccessful decision-makers. The importance of
intellectual courage, self-criticism, and holistic thinking.
- **Chapters**: 10-11

### 6. Learning from Failure
How to build institutions and decision processes that
compensate for cognitive limitations.
- **Chapters**: 12

## Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| Ballistic Decision | One-shot decision without
feedback adjustment |
| Oversteering | Excessive corrective action due to
ignoring delays |
| Thematic Vagabonding | Jumping between problems without
resolving any |
| Encapsulation | Isolating a problem from its context |
| Repair Service Behavior | Fixing symptoms rather than
causes |
| Failure of Anticipation | Not projecting side effects
and long-term consequences |
| Autocratic Attitude | Ignoring input; believing one
has full control |
| Degeneration of Goals | Losing sight of primary goals
in favor of sub-goals |
| Positive Feedback Trap | Initial success breeds
overconfidence |
| Nonlinearity | Disproportionate relationship between
cause and effect |
| Tanaland | Simulated African region used in
experiments |
| Lohhausen | Simulated town management experiment |
| Hypothesis Formation | Stating explicit assumptions
before acting |
| Temporal Integration | Considering long-term
consequences |
