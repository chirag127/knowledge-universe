## Author Background

Albert-Laszlo Barabasi (b. 1967) is a Hungarian-born network
scientist, Distinguished Professor at Northeastern
University, and the director of the Center for Complex
Network Research. He earned his Ph.D. from Boston University
(1994). He is most famous for the Barabasi-Albert (BA) model
of preferential attachment, which he published with Reka
Albert in *Science* (1999) — one of the most cited papers in
modern science. He has also written *Bursts* (2010) and
*The Formula* (2018). His research expanded into network
medicine — using network analysis to understand disease.

## Personal Notes

- The Barabasi-Albert model paper (1999) has been cited over
60,000 times — it essentially created the field of network
science.
- Barabasi was a physicist who applied statistical mechanics
to network structure — a paradigm shift from the graph-
theoretic approach.
- The "Achilles' heel" insight — scale-free networks are
robust yet fragile — is one of the most practically
consequential findings in systems theory.
- A limitation: subsequent research showed that not all real
networks are scale-free; the universality claim was
overstated. Clauset, Shalizi, and Newman (2009) developed
rigorous statistical tests showing many claimed power-law
distributions are not true power laws.
- The book's greatest strength is making complex network
mathematics accessible without equations.
- Network science has since become foundational in
epidemiology (COVID-19 spread modeling), social media
analysis, and neuroscience.
