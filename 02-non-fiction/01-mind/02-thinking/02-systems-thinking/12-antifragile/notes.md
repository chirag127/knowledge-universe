## Author Background

Nassim Nicholas Taleb (b. 1960) is a Lebanese-American
essayist, mathematical statistician, former trader, and
risk analyst. He is a Distinguished Professor at NYU's
Tandon School of Engineering. He spent 20 years as a
options trader before turning to writing and research.
The Incerto series — Fooled by Randomness (2001), The
Black Swan (2007), Antifragile (2012), The Bed of
Procrustes (2010), and Skin in the Game (2018) — is his
multi-volume philosophical essay on uncertainty,
probability, and decision-making. Taleb is known for his
polemical style, intellectual iconoclasm, and rejection of
establishment economics.

## Personal Notes

- The word "antifragile" did not exist before Taleb coined
it — he argues that English lacked a word for the opposite
of fragile because society has been conditioned to think
in binary (fragile vs. robust) rather than ternary.
- The concept has deep roots: Taleb draws on Stoic
philosophy (Seneca), biological evolution, and options
theory.
- The barbell strategy is one of the most practically
actionable ideas: avoid the middle, take extreme positions
in both safety and speculation.
- "Via Negativa" (the power of subtraction) connects to
simple heuristics, minimalist design, and the broader
principle of "less is more."
- Taleb's writing style is intentionally provocative and
digressive — he uses aphorisms, anecdotes, and personal
attacks on intellectual opponents. Some readers find this
engaging; others find it obnoxious.
- The "Green Lumber Fallacy" is one of the most subtle and
important concepts: people often succeed while holding
fundamentally wrong beliefs because they have useful
proxies.
- Antifragile is not strictly a systems thinking book in
the conventional sense, but its core contribution —
understanding how systems respond to stress — is essential
systems thinking.
- The book's main weakness is its lack of empirical
evidence for many claims; it relies heavily on anecdote
and thought experiment.
