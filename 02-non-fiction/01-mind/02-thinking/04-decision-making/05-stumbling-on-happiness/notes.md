### Author

**Daniel Gilbert** — Edgar Pierce Professor of Psychology at Harvard University. He is a pioneer in the field of affective forecasting (predicting future emotional states). His research has won numerous awards including the Distinguished Scientific Achievement Award from the Society for Experimental Social Psychology. His TED Talk on happiness is one of the most viewed of all time.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- The impact bias: we consistently overestimate how bad we'll feel after negative events
- The psychological immune system is incredibly powerful — we cope far better than we expect
- The best way to predict future happiness is not to imagine it — ask someone who's already done it
- Presentism explains why we buy too much food when hungry and too much clothing when cold
- We don't learn from our forecasting mistakes because we misremember our predictions
- "Happiness is not really what or where we thought it was"

### Strengths
- Exceptionally witty, humorous, and engaging prose
- Deeply original synthesis of cognitive science, social psychology, and neuroscience
- Rigorous research presented with extraordinary clarity
- The three-error framework is elegant and memorable

### Limitations
- Solutions are thin — the book diagnoses brilliantly but offers fewer prescriptions
- The humor, while delightful, occasionally overwhelms the substance
- Some found the tone overly glib about serious topics (tragedy, loss)
- The book focuses on individual prediction errors with less attention to social/systemic solutions

### Key Research Referenced
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases, prospect theory)
- Brickman & Campbell (hedonic adaptation, lottery winners/paraplegics)
- Wilson & Gilbert (affective forecasting, affective immunity)
- Loewenstein & Van Boven (hot/cold empathy gaps)
- Hsee & Bazerman (comparison, evaluability)
- Thaler & Sunstein (nudge theory)
- Schwarz & Strack (mood measurement)

### Cultural Impact
- New York Times Bestseller (weeks on the list)
- Translated into 30+ languages
- Gilbert's TED Talk "Why We Make Bad Decisions" has millions of views
- Helped establish affective forecasting as a field
- Frequently cited in behavioral economics, psychology, and public policy
