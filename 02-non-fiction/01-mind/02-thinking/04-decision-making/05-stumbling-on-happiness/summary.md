## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

The human brain possesses a remarkable ability: it can travel through time, imagining future scenarios and predicting how they will make us feel. But this ability is fundamentally flawed. We make three systematic errors when imagining the future: we fill in missing details with present reality (presentism), we fail to notice that details are missing (realism), and we underestimate our own psychological defenses (rationalization). As a result, we are consistently wrong about what will make us happy — and we don't know we're wrong.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. Prospection — The Brain's Time Travel Machine
The uniquely human ability to imagine and simulate future events. Gilbert argues this is the brain's most important function — not memory, but prospection.

### 2. The Three Flaws of Imagination
- **Realism** (Part III): We imagine futures that look like movies — but our imaginations leave out details and add nonexistent ones
- **Presentism** (Part IV): We project current feelings onto the future, failing to account for how we'll change
- **Rationalization** (Part V): We underestimate our psychological immune system — our ability to make the best of bad situations

### 3. Affective Forecasting Errors
We make four specific errors when predicting future feelings:
- **Impact bias**: We overestimate the intensity and duration of future emotions
- **Focalism**: We focus on one event and ignore everything else that will also happen
- **Presentism**: Current state colors future predictions
- **Immune neglect**: We forget we'll rationalize and adapt

### 4. The Psychological Immune System
The brain's remarkable capacity to construct positive views of reality — even in the face of tragedy. We are far more resilient than we think.

### 5. Subjective Well-Being Measurement
How psychologists measure happiness — and the surprising finding that people are often wrong about what they feel and will feel.

### 6. Corrigibility — Learning from Experience
We learn less from experience than we think because we don't accurately remember our own predictions or outcomes.

## 📋 Quick Reference Techniques Table

| Error | Description | Mitigation |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| **Impact Bias** | Overestimating intensity/duration of emotions | Ask "How long will this really affect me?" |
| **Focalism** | Focusing on one event, ignoring all else | Consider the whole context — not just the event |
| **Presentism** | Projecting current state onto future | Wait before deciding; consult others |
| **Immune Neglect** | Forgetting we'll rationalize | Trust that you'll cope better than you think |
| **Realism (Imagination gaps)** | Imagining incomplete futures | Use past experience, not imagination alone |
| **Memory Errors** | Basing predictions on flawed memories | Keep records; consult objective data |
| **Social Comparison Errors** | Wrong assumptions about others' happiness | Use surrogates: ask people who've done it |
