### Author

**Thomas E. Kida** — Professor Emeritus of Accounting at the Isenberg School of Management, University of Massachusetts Amherst. He has published extensively on judgment and decision-making, particularly in auditing contexts. His research focuses on how cognitive biases affect professional judgment.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- We naturally prefer vivid stories over cold statistics — this is our #1 thinking mistake
- Confirmation bias (seeking to confirm) is the most dangerous for professionals
- We see patterns everywhere, including where none exist
- Eyewitness memory is shockingly unreliable
- The scientific method is the best corrective for all six mistakes
- Social influence can override even well-developed critical thinking

### Strengths
- Clear, accessible framework (the six-pack of problems)
- Broad coverage from perception to memory to social influence
- Well-chosen examples from everyday life
- Strong emphasis on scientific thinking as the solution
- Good balance of theory and practical application

### Limitations
- Less original than some other books in this category (synthesizes existing ideas)
- The "six-pack" framework doesn't cleanly map to all 12 chapters
- Some chapters (e.g., framing) cover ground that overlaps heavily with other books
- Less depth on each topic compared to specialized treatments
- The writing style is competent but less engaging than Gilbert or Ariely

### Domains Covered
- Psychological research on memory, perception, and cognition
- Behavioral economics (framing, anchoring, decision biases)
- Social psychology (conformity, authority, groupthink)
- Philosophy of science (falsifiability, scientific method)
- Probability and statistics (chance, regression to the mean)

### Notable References
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases)
- Gilovich (How We Know What Isn't So — a clear influence)
- Shermer (Why People Believe Weird Things)
- Asch, Milgram, Zimbardo (social influence)
- Loftus (false memory, eyewitness testimony)
- Sagan (The Demon-Haunted World — scientific skepticism)
