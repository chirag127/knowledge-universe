### Authors

**Max H. Bazerman** — Jesse Isidor Straus Professor of Business Administration at Harvard Business School. Co-director of the Center for Public Leadership at Harvard Kennedy School. One of the world's leading experts on managerial decision-making, negotiation, and ethics. Author of *The Power of Noticing* (2014) and many other books.

**Don A. Moore** — Professor of Management at the Haas School of Business, University of California, Berkeley. His research focuses on overconfidence, competition, and decision-making. Author of *Perfectly Confident* (2020).

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- Overconfidence is the most pervasive and dangerous managerial bias
- Bounded awareness: we miss important information that is right in front of us
- Framing effects are powerful enough to reverse preferences
- Escalation of commitment is one of the most costly biases in organizations
- Bounded ethicality explains why good people make unethical decisions
- Debiasing is possible but requires deliberate effort and structural support

### Strengths
- Comprehensive, research-grounded coverage of the bias literature
- Clear organization from diagnosis to solutions
- Each chapter builds logically on the previous
- Strong emphasis on practical application for managers
- Balances depth with accessibility for a textbook

### Limitations
- Academic tone — less engaging than popular books on the same topics
- Heavy focus on what goes wrong (biases) rather than what goes right (expertise)
- Some content overlaps significantly with Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
- Solutions chapter is relatively thin compared to the diagnosis
- The 8th edition is somewhat dated now (2012)

### Key Research Referenced
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases, prospect theory)
- Tversky & Kahneman (framing, preferences)
- Ariely (emotional influences)
- Ross & Staw (escalation of commitment)
- Bazerman's own research (negotiation, ethics)
- Bandura (moral disengagement)
- Chugh & Bazerman (bounded awareness, bounded ethicality)

### Editions History
- First edition: 1986 (Bazerman only)
- Multiple editions through 8th (2012)
- The 8th edition added Moore as co-author
- Each edition has updated research and examples
