## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Managers are systematically biased in ways that undermine their judgment and decision-making. These biases — overconfidence, anchoring, availability, framing, escalation of commitment, and many others — are not random errors but predictable patterns. By understanding the psychological roots of these biases, learning to recognize them in organizational contexts, and applying structured decision processes, managers can significantly improve the quality of their judgments and decisions.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. Systematic Cognitive Biases
An extensive catalog of biases organized by type: overconfidence, heuristics (availability, representativeness, anchoring), framing, and group biases.

### 2. Bounded Awareness
The systematic failure to notice important information that is in one's visual or cognitive field. Related to inattentional blindness.

### 3. Framing and Preference Reversals
How the presentation of information — gains vs. losses, reference points, and context — systematically changes choices.

### 4. Motivational and Emotional Influences
The impact of incentives, emotions, and self-interest on judgment. The tension between "should" and "want" selves.

### 5. Escalation of Commitment
The tendency to continue investing in a failing course of action. Root causes: self-justification, sunk costs, overoptimism.

### 6. Fairness and Ethics
How ethical blind spots, motivated blindness, and bounded ethicality affect managerial decisions.

### 7. Decision Improvement Strategies
Techniques for debiasing: considering the opposite, accountability, training, and structured processes.

| **Framing** | Gain/loss, Reference point shifts | Reframe decisions; use multiple frames |

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **Bounded Awareness** | The systematic failure to notice important information that is observable |
| **Overconfidence** | Excessive certainty about the accuracy of one's beliefs |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Seeking and interpreting evidence that supports existing beliefs |
| **Anchoring** | Over-reliance on the first piece of information offered |
| **Availability Heuristic** | Judging event frequency by how easily examples come to mind |
| **Representativeness** | Judging probability by similarity to stereotypes |
| **Framing** | How the presentation of options affects choices |
| **Escalation of Commitment** | Continuing a failing course of action |
| **Hindsight Bias** | Overestimating predictability of past events |
| **Sunk Cost** | Treating irretrievable past investments as relevant to current decisions |
| **Fixed-Pie Bias** | Assuming negotiations are zero-sum when they aren't |
| **Reactive Devaluation** | Devaluing proposals from opponents simply because of their source |
| **Bounded Ethicality** | The systematic tendency to act against our own ethical standards unwittingly |
| **Motivated Blindness** | Failing to notice unethical behavior when noticing would conflict with self-interest |
