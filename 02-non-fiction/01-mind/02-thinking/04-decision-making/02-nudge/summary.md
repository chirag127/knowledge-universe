## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Human beings are not the rational "Econs" of classical economics — we are predictably irrational Humans with systematic biases. Rather than trying to eliminate these biases, we can design choice environments ("choice architecture") that gently nudge people toward better decisions while preserving their freedom of choice — a philosophy called "libertarian paternalism."

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. Humans vs. Econs
- **Econs**: Fictional beings who always make rational, optimized choices
- **Humans**: Real people with bounded rationality, limited willpower, and social susceptibility
- Policy and systems should be designed for Humans, not Econs

### 2. Choice Architecture
Every aspect of how choices are presented — the order of options, the default setting, the framing — influences decisions. There is no neutral way to present choices.

### 3. Libertarian Paternalism
- **Libertarian**: Preserve freedom of choice; make it easy to opt out
- **Paternalistic**: Steer people toward decisions that improve their welfare
- The apparent oxymoron is resolved by preserving choice while guiding outcomes

### 4. NUDGES Acronym
- i**N**centives — Align incentives properly
- **U**nderstanding — Map choices to outcomes comprehensibly
- **D**efaults — Leverage inertia through smart defaults
- **G**ive Feedback — Provide timely information about outcomes
- **E**xpect Error — Design forgiving systems that anticipate human mistakes
- **S**tructure Complex Choices — Use categorization and decision aids

### 5. The Power of Defaults
Because of inertia, whatever option is preselected will be chosen by a majority. Defaults are the most powerful nudge.

### 6. Social Influences
Humans are herd-followers. We look to others for cues about what is correct. This can be harnessed to nudge better behavior.

### 7. The Save More Tomorrow Plan
A program that commits employees to future savings increases, tied to pay raises — overcomes both inertia and loss aversion.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **Nudge** | Any aspect of choice architecture that alters behavior predictably without forbidding options or changing incentives significantly |
| **Choice Architecture** | The environment in which decisions are made and how options are presented |
| **Libertarian Paternalism** | Guiding people toward better choices while preserving their freedom to choose otherwise |
| **Econs** | Hypothetical perfectly rational beings assumed by traditional economics |
| **Humans** | Real people with cognitive biases, limited willpower, and social tendencies |
| **Default** | The option that takes effect if no active choice is made |
| **Anchoring** | Over-relying on the first piece of information offered when making decisions |
| **Loss Aversion** | The tendency to prefer avoiding losses over acquiring equivalent gains |
| **Status Quo Bias** | Preference for the current state of affairs; inertia in decision making |
| **Herding** | Following the behavior of others, often uncritically |
| **Framing** | How the presentation of information affects choices |
| **Save More Tomorrow** | A commitment device where future raises automatically increase savings |
| **Bounded Rationality** | The limits on human cognitive capacity that prevent fully rational decision making |
| **Overconfidence** | Excessive confidence in one's own judgments and abilities |
