### Authors

**Richard H. Thaler** — Professor of Behavioral Science and Economics at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business. Winner of the 2017 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for his contributions to behavioral economics. Often called the "father of behavioral economics."

**Cass R. Sunstein** — Professor at Harvard Law School. Former Administrator of the White House Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (2009–2012). One of the most cited legal scholars in the United States.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- The concept of "choice architecture" — there is no neutral way to present choices
- Defaults are the most powerful nudge because of human inertia
- Libertarian paternalism is not an oxymoron: freedom + gentle guidance
- The Save More Tomorrow program is the most successful nudge in history
- Organ donation: the difference between opt-in and opt-out is life or death

### Strengths
- Foundational text that launched the behavioral economics policy movement
- Clear, accessible writing despite the academic credentials of the authors
- Strong balance of theory, evidence, and practical application
- Addresses objections honestly and thoroughly

### Limitations
- Some policy proposals (privatizing marriage, Social Security) are controversial and may date the book
- Less applicable to individual decision-making (focus is on system design)
- Some critics argue it underestimates the power of education and information
- The political framing has been criticized by both left (too market-friendly) and right (too paternalistic)

### Center for Public Leadership
- The book inspired the creation of the UK Behavioural Insights Team ("Nudge Unit") in 2010
- Similar teams now exist in the US, Australia, Germany, Singapore, and dozens of other countries
- Thaler's 2017 Nobel Prize cemented the book's legacy

### Notable References
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases)
- Daniel Gilbert (affective forecasting)
- Milgram, Asch (social influence experiments)
- John Stuart Mill (liberty)
- Adam Smith (the invisible hand)
