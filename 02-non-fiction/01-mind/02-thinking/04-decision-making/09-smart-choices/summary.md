## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Making smart choices is a learnable skill that follows a structured process. The PrOACT model — Problem, Objectives, Alternatives, Consequences, Tradeoffs — provides a systematic approach to any decision. Additional elements (Uncertainty, Risk Tolerance, Linked Decisions) handle complexity. The key is to avoid psychological traps and approach decisions proactively rather than reactively. By following this process, anyone can make consistently better decisions.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. The PrOACT Framework
The core eight-element model for decision-making:
- **P**roblem — Define the decision problem
- **O**bjectives — Clarify what you really want to achieve
- **A**lternatives — Create better alternatives to choose from
- **C**onsequences — Describe how well each alternative meets objectives
- **T**radeoffs — Make tough compromises
- **U**ncertainty — Account for what you don't know
- **R**isk Tolerance — Account for your appetite for risk
- **L**inked Decisions — Plan ahead by coordinating current and future decisions

### 2. Proactive vs. Reactive Decision-Making
Proactive decision-makers define their own problems and objectives rather than letting circumstances dictate their choices.

### 3. Creating Alternatives
The most valuable step. Generating more and better alternatives is the highest-leverage activity in decision-making.

### 4. Tradeoffs and Compromises
When objectives conflict (you can't have it all), systematic tradeoff methods (even swaps, scoring) help make the best choice.

### 5. Psychological Traps
Eight common traps including anchoring, status quo, confirming evidence, framing, overconfidence, prudence, recallability, and the base-rate fallacy.

### 6. Uncertainty Management
How to think about and act on uncertainties using probability, expected value, and scenario planning.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **PrOACT** | The eight-element decision-making framework |
| **Proactive Decision-Making** | Choosing to define and tackle problems rather than reacting to circumstances |
| **Means-Ends Objectives** | Distinguishing fundamental objectives (ends) from ways to achieve them (means) |
| **Alternatives** | The range of possible courses of action |
| **Consequences** | The outcomes associated with each alternative |
| **Tradeoffs** | The unavoidable reality that achieving one objective often comes at the expense of another |
| **Even Swap** | A method for making tradeoffs by adjusting one objective's value to match another |
| **Uncertainty** | Lack of information about future events affecting the decision |
| **Risk Tolerance** | The amount of risk a decision-maker is willing to accept |
| **Linked Decisions** | Current decisions that affect or constrain future decisions |
| **Decision Traps** | Systematic psychological biases that distort judgment |
| **Anchoring Trap** | Over-relying on the first piece of information received |
| **Status Quo Trap** | Bias toward maintaining the current situation |
| **Confirming Evidence Trap** | Seeking information that supports existing views |
