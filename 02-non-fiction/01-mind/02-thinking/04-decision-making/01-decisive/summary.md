## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Human decision-making is systematically flawed due to four "villains" (narrow framing, confirmation bias, short-term emotion, and overconfidence), but we can defeat them using the WRAP framework: **W**iden your options, **R**eality-test your assumptions, **A**ttain distance before deciding, and **P**repare to be wrong. By following this structured process, individuals and organizations can make more rational, farsighted, and ultimately better decisions.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. The Four Villains of Decision Making
- **Narrow Framing**: Tendency to define choices too restrictively (yes/no when more options exist)
- **Confirmation Bias**: Seeking information that supports our existing view
- **Short-term Emotion**: Getting hijacked by fleeting feelings
- **Overconfidence**: Excessive faith in our ability to predict the future

### 2. The WRAP Framework
- **W**iden Your Options — Fight narrow framing through multitracking and exploring
- **R**eality-Test Your Assumptions — Fight confirmation bias through opposite thinking
- **A**ttain Distance Before Deciding — Fight short-term emotion through perspective-shifting
- **P**repare to Be Wrong — Fight overconfidence through premortems and tripwires

### 3. Multitracking
Considering multiple options simultaneously rather than sequentially, which produces better decisions and more creative solutions.

### 4. The Ooch Principle
Running small experiments (ooching) to test assumptions before committing fully — analogous to a diver testing the water temperature with a toe.

### 5. Vanishing Options Bias
The tendency to consider only options that are immediately visible, overlooking the possibility of creating new alternatives.

### 6. Tripwires
Setting predetermined signals that alert you when it's time to reconsider a decision, protecting against inertia and overcommitment.

### 7. Core Priorities
Using long-term values and priorities to evaluate decisions rather than being swayed by short-term emotional reactions.

### 8. Bookending the Future
Considering both best-case and worst-case scenarios to combat overconfidence and prepare for a range of outcomes.
