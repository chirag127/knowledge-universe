### Author

**Sheena Iyengar** — S. T. Lee Professor of Business at Columbia Business School. Born blind, she has become one of the world's leading researchers on choice and decision-making. Her famous "jam study" (comparing 6 vs. 24 varieties) is one of the most cited experiments in behavioral economics. She was named one of the "Top 50 Thinkers" by Forbes.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- The jam study: 6 options sell better than 24 — the most famous experiment in choice overload
- Collectivist cultures often prefer choices made BY others (family, community)
- Unconscious sensory factors (lighting, scent, sound) dramatically affect choices
- We have less conscious control over our choices than we believe
- Both too much choice AND too little choice are harmful — the sweet spot varies
- Perceived control is as important as actual control

### Strengths
- Uniquely blends personal narrative with rigorous research
- Cultural perspective is a vital corrective to Western-centric assumptions
- Beautiful, literary writing style
- Iyengar's personal story of blindness gives her extraordinary credibility and insight

### Limitations
- Less prescriptive than diagnostic — fewer actionable tools
- Some chapters feel more like intellectual exploration than focused argument
- The cultural analysis may oversimplify complex cultural differences
- The autobiographical elements, while compelling, slow the argument

### Key Research Referenced
- Iyengar & Lepper (jam study, choice overload)
- Iyengar & DeVoe (cultural differences in choice)
- Iyengar & Brodscholl (choice and autonomy)
- Kahneman & Tversky (framing, biases)
- Seligman & Maier (learned helplessness)
- Langer & Rodin (control in nursing homes)
- Nisbett & Wilson (introspection limits)

### Cultural Impact
- The jam study is arguably the most famous finding in behavioral economics
- Influenced product assortment design in retail
- Widely referenced in psychology, marketing, and public policy
- Iyengar's TED Talks on choice have millions of views
