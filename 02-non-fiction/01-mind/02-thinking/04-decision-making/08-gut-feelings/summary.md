## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

Our gut feelings are not mystical intuitions or primitive impulses — they are the result of evolved and learned heuristics that exploit the structure of our environment. Under conditions of uncertainty (where not all information is known), less information and simpler rules can actually lead to better decisions than complex analysis. The unconscious mind uses these "fast and frugal" heuristics to make smart decisions quickly, and understanding when to trust these gut feelings is the key to better real-world decision-making.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. Fast and Frugal Heuristics
The mind uses simple rules of thumb that ignore most information — and this ignoring is a feature, not a bug. Less is often more.

### 2. The Intelligence of the Unconscious
Gut feelings are not irrational — they reflect the adaptive wisdom of evolved and learned cognitive processes operating outside awareness.

### 3. The Recognition Heuristic
If one of two objects is recognized and the other is not, infer that the recognized object has the higher value on the criterion. This simple rule often beats complex analysis.

### 4. The Take-the-Best Heuristic
Search cues in order of validity; stop at the first cue that discriminates; ignore all other cues. One good reason is enough.

### 5. Less Is More
More information, more time, and more computation can actually reduce decision accuracy. Simplicity can outperform complexity.

### 6. Ecological Rationality
A heuristic is not good or bad in itself — it depends on the environment. Heuristics are rational when they match the structure of the environment.

### 7. Innate vs. Learned Intuitions
Some gut feelings are evolved (fear of heights, snake avoidance). Others are learned through experience (expert intuition).

### 8. Moral and Social Intuitions
Our moral judgments and social behaviors are largely intuitive, arising from evolved social instincts.

| **Take-the-Best** | Using the single most valid cue that discriminates between options |
