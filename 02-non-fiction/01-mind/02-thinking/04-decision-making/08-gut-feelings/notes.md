### Author

**Gerd Gigerenzer** — Director of the Max Planck Institute for Human Development in Berlin and Director of the Harding Center for Risk Literacy. He is the leading advocate of the "fast and frugal heuristics" approach to decision-making, which argues that heuristics are adaptive tools rather than sources of error. He is a prominent critic of the Kahneman/Tversky heuristics-and-biases program. His other books include *Risk Savvy* (2014) and *Simple Heuristics That Make Us Smart* (1999, co-authored).

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- Heuristics are not bugs — they are features of an adaptive mind
- Less information can produce better decisions: the less-is-more effect
- The recognition heuristic is remarkably powerful and often accurate
- Ecological rationality: the quality of a heuristic depends on the environment
- Gut feelings are neither mysterious nor primitive — they are intelligent
- Statistical intuitions can be improved by using natural frequencies (not percentages)
- Medical decision-making is a domain where simple rules often beat complex models

### Strengths
- Powerful counter-argument to the "humans are irrational" narrative
- Clear, evidence-based demonstrations of heuristic success
- The ecological rationality framework is philosophically robust
- Practical applications in health care and risk communication
- Well-structured from theory to application

### Limitations
- Sometimes overstates the case for heuristics against analytical methods
- The debate with Kahneman can overshadow the book's own contributions
- Some heuristics (take-the-best) work only in specific environments
- Less applicable to organizational or group decision-making contexts
- May understate the real dangers of bias in some situations

### Key Research Referenced
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases) — critical dialogue
- Simon (bounded rationality, satisficing)
- Cosmides & Tooby (evolutionary psychology, cheater detection)
- Haidt (moral intuition, social intuitionism)
- Goldstein & Gigerenzer (recognition heuristic)
- Martignon & Hoffrage (fast and frugal trees)

### The Gigerenzer-Kahneman Debate
- Gigerenzer and Kahneman represent the two poles of modern decision science
- Kahneman: heuristics are shortcuts that lead to systematic bias
- Gigerenzer: heuristics are adaptive tools that exploit environmental structure
- The debate has been productive for the field, clarifying the conditions under which intuition works vs. fails
