# Gut Feelings: The Intelligence of the Unconscious — Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

## Reading Guidance

The book has two parts. Part I (Ch 1-6) provides the theoretical foundation: what gut feelings are, how intuition works, and why they shouldn't be logical. Part II (Ch 7-11) shows gut feelings in action across domains: choice, health care, morality, and social behavior.

---

## Part I: Unconscious Intelligence

### Chapter 1: Gut Feelings

**Core Question**: What are gut feelings, and why should we trust them?

**What it Covers**: Introduction to the concept of gut feelings — judgments that appear quickly in consciousness without awareness of the underlying processes. The adaptive value of intuition.

**Key Concepts**: Gut feelings, unconscious intelligence, intuition, tacit knowledge

**Examples**: A firefighter who "senses" a building will collapse; a tennis player who knows where the ball will go.

**Takeaway**: Gut feelings are intelligent — they reflect learning and evolution, not error.

**Thesis Connection**: The foundational claim — gut feelings are smart.

---

### Chapter 2: Less Is (Sometimes) More

**Core Question**: When does having less information lead to better decisions?

**What it Covers**: The less-is-more effect. More information, computation, and time can actually degrade decision quality. Simplicity often beats complexity.

**Key Concepts**: Less-is-more, information overload, parsimony, Ockham's razor

**Examples**: Studies showing that simple heuristics predict better than complex statistical models in certain environments.

**Takeaway**: Knowing when to ignore information is a key decision skill.

**Thesis Connection**: The counter-intuitive core of Gigerenzer's argument.

---

### Chapter 3: How Intuition Works

**Core Question**: What is the mechanism behind gut feelings?

**What it Covers**: The mechanics of intuition — fast and frugal heuristics. Rules of thumb that use limited information. The adaptive toolbox.

**Key Concepts**: Fast and frugal heuristics, adaptive toolbox, bounded rationality

**Examples**: The recognition heuristic; the take-the-best heuristic.

**Takeaway**: Intuition operates through simple rules that exploit environmental structure.

**Thesis Connection**: The mechanistic explanation of how gut feelings work.

---

### Chapter 4: Evolved Brains

**Core Question**: What gut feelings did we inherit from evolution?

**What it Covers**: Evolved intuitions — fears, preferences, and social instincts shaped by natural selection. The brain's evolved architecture for quick decisions.

**Key Concepts**: Evolved intuition, modular brain, prepared learning, evolutionary psychology

**Examples**: Fear of heights and snakes; preference for sweet foods; social intuitions.

**Takeaway**: Some gut feelings are innate — products of evolution.

**Thesis Connection**: Biological foundation for intuitive intelligence.

---

### Chapter 5: Adapted Minds

**Core Question**: How does learning shape intuition?

**What it Covers**: Learned intuitions — gut feelings acquired through experience. How practice, feedback, and immersion create expert intuition.

**Key Concepts**: Learning, expertise, perceptual learning, automaticity

**Examples**: Chess masters; radiologists recognizing tumors; experienced drivers.

**Takeaway**: Most human intuition is learned, not innate — it develops with experience.

**Thesis Connection**: Experience-based learning is the other source of intuitive intelligence.

---

### Chapter 6: Why Good Intuitions Shouldn't Be Logical

**Core Question**: Why does intuition violate the rules of logic yet still be correct?

**What it Covers**: Logic and probability theory are not always the right standards for judging intuition. Ecological rationality: a judgment is rational if it fits the environment.

**Key Concepts**: Ecological rationality, logical vs. ecological, coherence vs. correspondence

**Examples**: Conjunction fallacies that turn out to be good judgments in real-world contexts.

**Takeaway**: Good intuitions often violate formal logic but make perfect sense in context.

**Thesis Connection**: The philosophical foundation — redefining rationality itself.

---

## Part II: Gut Feelings in Action

### Chapter 7: Ever Heard Of...?

**Core Question**: How does recognition guide our choices?

**What it Covers**: The recognition heuristic in action. Using recognition as a cue for value. Applications in marketing, investing, and everyday decisions.

**Key Concepts**: Recognition heuristic, mere exposure, brand recognition

**Examples**: The "most successful mutual fund" study — investors chose recognized names (and did well); Americans' and Germans' city rankings.

**Takeaway**: Recognition is a powerful, often reliable heuristic — but it can be exploited.

**Thesis Connection**: First major applied demonstration of a fast and frugal heuristic.

---

### Chapter 8: One Good Reason Is Enough

**Core Question**: How do we decide when multiple factors compete?

**What it Covers**: The take-the-best heuristic and its competitors. How the mind makes choices with multiple cues by using the single best discriminating cue.

**Key Concepts**: Take-the-best, cue validity, stopping rule, lexicographic strategies

**Examples**: Studies showing take-the-best predicts better than multiple regression in some environments.

**Takeaway**: Searching for many reasons can be counterproductive — one good reason is often enough.

**Thesis Connection**: Most important specific heuristic — demonstrates the less-is-more principle.

---

### Chapter 9: Less Is More in Health Care

**Core Question**: How can intuitive heuristics improve medical decisions?

**What it Covers**: Applications of fast and frugal heuristics in medicine. Teaching doctors and patients to use simple decision trees. Statistical intuition and risk communication.

**Key Concepts**: Medical decision making, risk communication, natural frequencies, decision trees

**Examples**: The "GRACE" heart attack risk score (simple tree beats complex model); mammogram screening risk communication.

**Takeaway**: Simple decision rules can outperform complex models in medical diagnosis and treatment.

**Thesis Connection**: The most high-stakes application of the less-is-more principle.

---

### Chapter 10: Moral Behavior

**Core Question**: Are our moral judgments intuitive or reasoned?

**What it Covers**: Moral intuitions — automatic judgments of right and wrong. The role of emotion in moral decisions. Moral heuristics.

**Key Concepts**: Moral intuition, disgust, harm avoidance, reciprocity, moral heuristics

**Examples**: Haidt's moral dumbfounding; trolley problems; disgust and purity judgments.

**Takeaway**: Most moral judgments are made intuitively, with reason providing post-hoc justifications.

**Thesis Connection**: Extends the gut feeling argument to moral domain.

---

### Chapter 11: Social Instincts

**Core Question**: How do social intuitions guide our interactions?

**What it Covers**: Social heuristics — rules of thumb for social life: fairness, gossip, cheater detection, imitation. The evolutionary foundation of social intelligence.

**Key Concepts**: Social intuition, cheater detection, reciprocity, fairness instinct, imitation

**Examples**: Ultimatum game; trust game; gossip as a social tool.

**Takeaway**: Our social gut feelings are sophisticated tools for navigating complex group life.

**Thesis Connection**: Completes the picture — intuition guides both individual and social decisions.
