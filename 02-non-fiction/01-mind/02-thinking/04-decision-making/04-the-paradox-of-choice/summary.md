## 💡 The Big Idea in One Paragraph

In modern consumer societies, we assume more choice leads to more freedom and greater satisfaction. But beyond a certain point, an abundance of choices creates paralysis, anxiety, regret, and dissatisfaction. The key to happiness is learning to be a "satisficer" who settles for "good enough" rather than a "maximizer" who must always have the best, and to embrace constraints, gratitude, and lowered expectations.

## 🔑 Thematic Pillars & Core Frameworks

### 1. Maximizers vs. Satisficers
- **Maximizers**: Seek the best possible option; compare relentlessly; experience more regret
- **Satisficers**: Settle for "good enough"; compare less; report higher life satisfaction
- This is a continuum, not a binary — but the distinction predicts well-being

### 2. The Problem of Regret
More options = more counterfactuals = more regret. Anticipated regret affects decisions before they're made; post-decision regret diminishes satisfaction.

### 3. Opportunity Costs and Tradeoffs
More options mean more tradeoffs, and each tradeoff comes with an opportunity cost that diminishes the subjective experience of the chosen option.

### 4. Adaptation and the Hedonic Treadmill
We adapt to almost everything, good and bad. The thrill of a new purchase fades, but we don't anticipate this adaptation, leading us to over-invest in acquisition.

### 5. Social Comparison
Evaluating our choices against others fuels dissatisfaction. Maximizers are especially vulnerable to the negative effects of social comparison.

### 6. The Psychology of Happiness
True well-being requires choosing when to choose — being selective about decision-making energy — rather than trying to optimize every choice.

## 🧠 Key Concepts Glossary

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| **Maximizer** | Someone who seeks the absolute best option in every decision |
| **Satisficer** | Someone who seeks a "good enough" option and stops searching |
| **Choice Overload** | The phenomenon where more options reduce decision quality and satisfaction |
| **Regret Aversion** | The tendency to avoid decisions due to fear of future regret |
| **Opportunity Cost** | The value of the next-best option foregone |
| **Counterfactual** | Imagined alternative to what actually happened |
| **Hedonic Adaptation** | The tendency to return to a baseline happiness level after events |
| **Social Comparison** | Evaluating oneself relative to others |
| **Self-Blame** | Taking personal responsibility for poor outcomes |
| **Framing Effects** | How the presentation of options affects choice |
| **Autonomy** | The sense of control over one's life decisions |
| **Maximization Scale** | Schwartz's psychometric measure of maximizing tendency |
