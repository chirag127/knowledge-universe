### Author

**Barry Schwartz** — Dorwin Cartwright Professor Emeritus of Social Theory and Social Action at Swarthmore College. His research focuses on the intersection of psychology and economics, especially the relationship between choice and happiness. He is also the author of *Practical Wisdom* (2010) and *Why We Work* (2015).

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- The maximizer/satisficer distinction is the book's most powerful idea — it predicts life satisfaction
- More options do NOT mean more freedom to a maximizer — they mean more to worry about
- "Good enough" is actually better for happiness than "the best"
- Anticipated regret before a decision and post-decision regret both reduce satisfaction
- Making decisions irreversible increases satisfaction (because we stop second-guessing)
- Social comparison is toxic — and abundant choice makes it worse

### Strengths
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized argument
- Grounded in rigorous psychological research
- The maximizer-satisficer scale is a useful diagnostic tool
- The topic is universally relevant and immediately recognizable

### Limitations
- Solutions chapter is relatively thin compared to the diagnosis
- Some examples feel dated (2004 technology references)
- Assumes a primarily American/consumerist context
- Understates the genuine benefits of choice for some populations and contexts
- May overgeneralize from consumer choice to all life decisions

### Key Research Referenced
- Sheena Iyengar's jam study (6 vs. 24 varieties)
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases, prospect theory)
- Brickman & Campbell (hedonic treadmill)
- Diener & Diener (subjective well-being research)
- Taylor & Brown (positive illusions)

### Cultural Impact
- "Choice overload" entered the popular lexicon
- The jam study is one of the most cited studies in behavioral economics
- Influenced product design (fewer SKUs), user experience, and public policy
- Preceded and informed the "minimalism" movement
