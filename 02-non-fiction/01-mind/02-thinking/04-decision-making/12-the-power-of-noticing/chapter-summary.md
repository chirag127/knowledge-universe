# The Power of Noticing — Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

## Reading Guidance

The book is structured as 12 chapters without formal parts, but chapters cluster thematically. Ch 1-4 establish the core concepts (bounded awareness, motivated blindness). Ch 5-8 explore specific failures of noticing (misdirection, slippery slope, missing data, implausible claims). Ch 9-12 focus on solutions (thinking ahead, indirect harm, leadership, capacity development).

---

## Preface: Noticing — A Personal Journey

**Core Question**: What motivated the author to write about noticing?

**What it Covers**: Bazerman's personal journey from studying cognitive biases to focusing on what leaders fail to notice. The inspiration from his MBA teaching.

**Key Concepts**: Personal narrative, bounded awareness origins

**Examples**: Teaching the Challenger case to executives; their consistent failure to ask the right questions.

**Takeaway**: Even very smart leaders miss obvious information — and this is fixable.

**Thesis Connection**: Establishes the real-world importance of noticing.

---

## Chapter 1: Racing and Fixing Cars

**Core Question**: Why do leaders fail to ask for additional information?

**What it Covers**: A decision-making simulation Bazerman uses with executives — deciding whether to race given uncertain conditions. Most focus on available data and fail to ask what's missing.

**Key Concepts**: Bounded awareness, available vs. missing information, the racing simulation

**Examples**: The Challenger disaster — engineers had the data but didn't ask the right question.

**Takeaway**: The first failure of noticing: accepting the information you have without asking what you're missing.

**Thesis Connection**: Introduction to the central concept — bounded awareness.

---

## Chapter 2: Motivated Blindness

**Core Question**: Why don't leaders see unethical behavior that is right in front of them?

**What it Covers**: Motivated blindness — the tendency to overlook unethical behavior when noticing would be costly or inconvenient to the observer.

**Key Concepts**: Motivated blindness, self-interest, conflict of interest

**Examples**: Auditors missing client fraud; sports officials missing home-team violations; the Enron case.

**Takeaway**: Self-interest systematically blinds us to ethical violations.

**Thesis Connection**: A major mechanism of failed noticing.

---

## Chapter 3: When Our Leaders Don't Notice

**Core Question**: How do failures of noticing cascade through organizations?

**What it Covers**: When leaders fail to notice, it creates systemic failures. Case studies of leadership failures rooted in inattention.

**Key Concepts**: Leadership blind spots, organizational noticing, accountability

**Examples**: The Challenger disaster; failures leading to the 2008 financial crisis.

**Takeaway**: Leaders' failures of noticing are amplified across organizations.

**Thesis Connection**: Organizational consequences of individual noticing failures.

---

## Chapter 4: Industrywide Blindness

**Core Question**: How can entire industries miss the obvious?

**What it Covers**: The phenomenon of industrywide blindness — entire sectors failing to notice critical information. Groupthink and shared blind spots.

**Key Concepts**: Industrywide blindness, groupthink, paradigm blindness, collective blind spots

**Examples**: The financial industry missing the housing bubble; the music industry missing digital disruption.

**Takeaway**: The most dangerous blind spots are the ones shared by everyone in an industry.

**Thesis Connection**: Systemic level of noticing failure.

---

## Chapter 5: What Do Magicians, Thieves, Advertisers, Politicians, and Negotiators Have in Common?

**Core Question**: How does misdirection work in different domains?

**What it Covers**: Misdirection — the deliberate focusing of attention away from important information. Lessons from magicians, thieves, and negotiators.

**Key Concepts**: Misdirection, attention management, framing, anchoring

**Examples**: Magicians' techniques; how negotiators use strategic misdirection; political manipulation.

**Takeaway**: Understanding how others exploit our blind spots helps us defend against manipulation.

**Thesis Connection**: External exploitation of bounded awareness.

---

## Chapter 6: Missing the Obvious on a Slippery Slope

**Core Question**: Why do we fail to notice gradual ethical decline?

**What it Covers**: The slippery slope — how ethical violations that happen incrementally are invisible at each step but catastrophic in aggregate.

**Key Concepts**: Slippery slope, incrementalism, ethical fading, normalization of deviance

**Examples**: The Challenger (O-rings over time); BP's safety culture erosion; personal ethical lapses.

**Takeaway**: Gradual change is the hardest to notice — set clear thresholds in advance.

**Thesis Connection**: Temporal dimension of noticing failure.

---

## Chapter 7: The Dog That Didn't Bark

**Core Question**: How do we learn from what isn't there?

**What it Covers**: The importance of noticing missing information. The Sherlock Holmes principle — the dog that didn't bark is itself a clue.

**Key Concepts**: Missing information, absent data, silence, signal detection

**Examples**: Holmes's "curious incident of the dog in the night-time"; missing warnings before 9/11.

**Takeaway**: What didn't happen is often as important as what did — train yourself to notice absence.

**Thesis Connection**: A key noticing skill — looking for what's missing.

---

## Chapter 8: There's Something Wrong with This Picture: Or, If It's Too Good to Be True...

**Core Question**: Why do smart people fall for too-good-to-be-true schemes?

**What it Covers**: The failure to scrutinize implausible claims. The Madoff case as the prime example.

**Key Concepts**: Too-good-to-be-true, implausibility, motivated skepticism, due diligence failure

**Examples**: Bernie Madoff's Ponzi scheme — many sophisticated investors failed to question the returns.

**Takeaway**: When something seems too good to be true, it probably is — leaders must be willing to ask uncomfortable questions.

**Thesis Connection**: A specific and costly failure of noticing.

---

## Chapter 9: Noticing by Thinking Ahead

**Core Question**: How do we anticipate what we might miss?

**What it Covers**: The value of proactive scenario planning. Using pre-mortems, what-if analysis, and strategic foresight to surface hidden risks.

**Key Concepts**: Thinking ahead, scenario planning, pre-mortem, strategic foresight

**Examples**: Shell's scenario planning for oil shocks; the US failure to anticipate 9/11.

**Takeaway**: Anticipating future scenarios helps us notice what we might otherwise miss.

**Thesis Connection**: The first major solution chapter — anticipatory noticing.

---

## Chapter 10: Failing to Notice Indirect Actions

**Core Question**: Why do we care less about harm we cause indirectly?

**What it Covers**: The systematic tendency to notice and care less about harm that is indirect or mediated. We see the direct effects of our actions but not the indirect chain of consequences.

**Key Concepts**: Direct vs. indirect harm, causal chains, moral distance, diffusion of responsibility

**Examples**: Factory farming (consumers don't see the harm); outsourcing pollution; systemic corruption.

**Takeaway**: Trace the full chain of consequences for your decisions — don't stop at the direct effects.

**Thesis Connection**: Noticing failure in the domain of moral responsibility.

---

## Chapter 11: Leadership to Avoid Predictable Surprises

**Core Question**: How can leaders prevent the crises they should have seen coming?

**What it Covers**: Predictable surprises — events that could have been anticipated but weren't. How leaders can create systems to surface emerging threats.

**Key Concepts**: Predictable surprise, early warning systems, organizational noticing, crisis prevention

**Examples**: 9/11 Commission findings; Hurricane Katrina; financial crises.

**Takeaway**: The best leaders don't just respond to crises — they anticipate and prevent them.

**Thesis Connection**: Leadership as the capacity to notice and act on emerging threats.

---

## Chapter 12: Developing the Capacity to Notice

**Core Question**: How can we train ourselves to notice more?

**What it Covers**: Practical strategies for developing noticing capacity: asking better questions, seeking disconfirming information, creating organizational structures for diverse perspectives, and cultivating intellectual humility.

**Key Concepts**: Noticing capacity, deliberate practice, organizational noticing design, intellectual humility

**Examples**: Specific exercises and organizational changes Bazerman recommends.

**Takeaway**: Noticing is a skill that can be developed through deliberate practice and organizational design.

**Thesis Connection**: The prescriptive conclusion — how to become someone who notices.
