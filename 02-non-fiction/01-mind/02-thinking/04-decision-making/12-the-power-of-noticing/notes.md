### Author

**Max H. Bazerman** — Jesse Isidor Straus Professor of Business Administration at Harvard Business School. Co-director of the Center for Public Leadership at Harvard Kennedy School. One of the world's leading experts on managerial decision-making, negotiation, and ethics. He has authored or co-authored over 200 research articles and several books including *Judgment in Managerial Decision Making* (8 editions), *Negotiation Genius*, and *The Power of Noticing*.

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- Bounded awareness is distinct from bounded rationality: it's about what we focus on vs. what we have capacity for
- Motivated blindness explains why good people in good organizations enable unethical behavior
- The slippery slope is the most dangerous ethical trap because it's invisible
- "The dog that didn't bark" is a powerful mental model for spotting missing information
- Too-good-to-be-true should trigger intense scrutiny, not excitement
- Noticing can be taught and developed — it's not a fixed trait
- Leaders who notice better prevent crises before they start

### Strengths
- Clear concept (bounded awareness) that extends Bazerman's academic work to a popular audience
- Excellent case studies (Challenger, Enron, Madoff, BP)
- Builds systematically from problem to solution
- Practical exercises and strategies in later chapters
- Accessible to both business and general readers

### Limitations
- Some chapters overlap significantly with Bazerman's earlier work (JMDM)
- Solutions are somewhat general — specific techniques are less developed than the diagnosis
- Less emphasis on individual/personal decision-making (focus is on leadership)
- Some cases are overused in the literature (Challenger appears in 4+ books in this category)
- The notion that leaders can simply "choose" to notice more understates organizational constraints

### Key Research Referenced
- Simons & Chabris (inattentional blindness / gorilla experiment)
- Bazerman's own research on bounded awareness, ethicality
- Kahneman & Tversky (attention, framing)
- Janis (groupthink)
- Vaughan (normalization of deviance, Challenger)
- Taleb (black swans, predictable surprises)

### Major Case Studies Used
- Challenger space shuttle disaster (Ch 1, 3, 6)
- Enron collapse (Ch 2)
- Bernie Madoff Ponzi scheme (Ch 8)
- BP Deepwater Horizon (Ch 6)
- 9/11 Commission findings (Ch 7, 11)
- Hurricane Katrina (Ch 11)
- Financial crisis of 2008 (Ch 4)
