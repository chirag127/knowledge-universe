### Author

**Gary Klein** — Senior Scientist at MacroCognition LLC. A pioneer of Naturalistic Decision Making (NDM). He has studied decision-making in the field for decades — firefighters, military commanders, nuclear power operators, and healthcare professionals. He co-developed the Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model and cognitive task analysis methods. His other books include *Streetlights and Shadows* (2009) and *Seeing What Others Don't* (2013).

## 📝 Personal Notes

### Key Takeaways
- The RPD model shows that experts don't compare options — they recognize and act
- Intuition is pattern recognition, not magic — and it can be trained
- Over-reliance on rational analysis ("hyperrationality") is a dangerous blind spot
- Stories are crucial cognitive tools for sense-making and knowledge transfer
- Team mind enables coordinated action without explicit communication
- Errors in natural settings come more from lack of experience/stress than from inherent bias

### Strengths
- Groundbreaking methodological contribution (field research over lab experiments)
- The firefighter studies provide vivid, compelling evidence
- Balanced view of intuition AND analysis (not anti-rational)
- Widely influential in military, healthcare, emergency response

### Limitations
- Dense academic style; less accessible than popular psychology books
- The RPD model can feel under-specified (how exactly does pattern recognition work?)
- Less attention to how novices become experts — the developmental pathway
- May understate the role of bias in expert decision-making
- The chapter-level detail can feel repetitive

### Key Concepts Explained
- Klein's work directly influenced Malcolm Gladwell's *Blink* (2005)
- Klein and Kahneman engaged in a famous intellectual debate about intuition (1999-2009)
- The RPD model is now used in military training, firefighting, nursing, and petrochemical operations
- Klein's cognitive task analysis methods are widely used in UX and system design

### Notable References
- Kahneman & Tversky (heuristics and biases) — Klein's foil
- Herbert Simon (bounded rationality, chess expertise)
- Dreyfus & Dreyfus (skill acquisition model)
- Chase & Simon (chess expertise)
